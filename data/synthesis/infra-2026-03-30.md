### Coverage: Q1 2023 – Q2 2026 | Companies with Full Data: 2 of 7

---

## 1. Candor Trajectory

**Sector pattern: Structural plateau with event-driven spikes. Neither company has improved candor durably.**

| Company | Baseline | Peak | Trough | Final | Direction |
|---------|----------|------|--------|-------|-----------|
| IREN | B– (2.635) | B+ (3.015, Q1 FY2026) | C+ (2.575, Q4 FY2024) | B (2.65) | Flat |
| CIFR | B (2.85) | B+ (3.43, Q1 2024) | C (2.35, Q4 2025) | B (2.80) | Flat with deterioration risk |

Both companies show an identical structural pattern: a baseline around B (2.65–2.85), one meaningful breakout above B+, and an immediate reversion. The breakouts are traceable to discrete events rather than improved communication discipline:

- **IREN's Q1 FY2026 spike** (3.015) was caused entirely by the Microsoft contract disclosure — named counterparty, $9.7B total value, 5-year term, prepayment structure, CapEx, and IRR range. The following quarter reverted to exactly 2.65. The Microsoft contract forced precision; management did not choose precision.

- **CIFR's Q1 2024 spike** (3.43) reflected exceptional financial specificity and an honest post-halving framing. It was followed by a sustained decline as the HPC pivot loaded aspirational content without contractual grounding. Q4 2025 hit a series low of 2.35, the worst call in the combined dataset.

**Outliers by direction:**
- *Improvement outlier:* Neither company. Both reverted.
- *Decline outlier:* CIFR Q4 2025 (2.35 / C grade) — a call framed as a "business update" that produced essentially no income statement, cash flow, or balance sheet data during an active multi-hundred-megawatt construction cycle. This is the single worst-graded quarter in the dataset.
- *Stagnation outlier:* IREN — seven of nine full-analysis quarters land at exactly 2.65. This is not random variation; it is a structural ceiling. The company has a revealed preference for a specific register of communication regardless of operational outcomes.

**Sector-level conclusion:** Neither company has demonstrated improving candor as a policy. Both have demonstrated that signed, named contracts produce better disclosure. The implication: candor quality in this sector is a function of what management is contractually required to disclose, not of communication culture.

## 3. Bottleneck Migration

With only two companies providing full data, bottleneck language cannot be tracked across a representative sector sample. The following reflects what CIFR and IREN disclosed:

**CIFR bottleneck language by period:**

| Period | Dominant Constraint |
|--------|-------------------|
| Q1–Q4 2023 | Power cost and availability (Odessa PPA as competitive moat; site selection = power access) |
| Q1–Q2 2024 | Miner availability and delivery timing (Bitmain contracts, S21 tranches) |
| Q3 2024–Q1 2025 | HPC tenant conversion (demand exists; deal closing is the constraint) |
| Q2 2025–Q1 2026 | Interconnect approvals and construction financing (ERCOT batch process; Fortress/AWS financing terms) |

**IREN bottleneck language by period:**

| Period | Dominant Constraint |
|--------|-------------------|
| Q3 FY2023–Q2 FY2024 | Miner acquisition and power capacity (Bitmain options; site power) |
| Q3 FY2024–Q4 FY2024 | AI customer conversion (Morgan Stanley process; Poolside departure) |
| Q1 FY2025–Q3 FY2025 | Power interconnection (2.3 GW signed; now the rate of energization is the limit) |
| Q4 FY2025–Q2 FY2026 | GPU procurement and financing (Microsoft GPU CapEx; $3.6B facility construction) |

**Cross-company migration signal:** Both companies independently show the same bottleneck sequence — power → miners/hardware → tenant conversion → financing/interconnect. The sector's constraint in 2023 was *supply-side* (getting power and hardware). By 2025–2026, the constraint is *transaction-side* (closing tenant agreements and securing construction capital). This is a meaningful shift: operational build-out risk is declining while commercial execution risk is rising. Neither company is currently capacity-constrained on power; both are execution-constrained on filling that capacity under contract.

**Absent from both companies' bottleneck language:** GPU chip supply, memory, and labor — the constraints dominating hyperscaler and data center developer discourse. This may reflect the companies' position in the stack (they are tenants or lessors, not GPU manufacturers) or genuine insulation from those constraints.

## 4. Cross-Company Corroboration

### Corroborated Signals (Both Companies Independently Confirm)

**1. Hyperscaler demand is real and at scale.**
CIFR signed AWS ($5.5B / 15 years) and Google (via Fluidstack, $3B / 10 years). IREN signed Microsoft ($9.7B / 5 years). Three of the five major hyperscalers appear as named counterparties across two companies. This is the strongest signal in the dataset — not one company's claim but two independent deal disclosures naming different hyperscalers. The demand is corroborated.

**2. Power-secured sites have structurally superior negotiating leverage.**
Both companies independently cite their pre-secured power positions as the primary reason hyperscalers are willing to sign. IREN's 2.3 GW of signed interconnection agreements is cited as the reason Microsoft selected them. CIFR's Odessa PPA and Black Pearl ERCOT position are cited as the reason AWS and Google are willing to commit. Neither company mentions GPU supply or compute as their primary offering — they are selling *energized land with grid access*.

**3. Large signed contracts do not resolve pipeline uncertainty.**
IREN signed Microsoft in Q1 FY2026 and immediately declared a $3.4B ARR target — with ~$1B uncontracted. CIFR signed AWS and Fluidstack/Google and immediately escalated its pipeline narrative to 3.2 GW — with no additional signed leases. Both companies exhibit the same pattern: a signed anchor contract is used to validate the entire pipeline as equally probable. It does not.

**4. Mining operations are being de-emphasized without formal strategy withdrawal.**
IREN management explicitly capped mining at 50 EH (a decision, not a constraint). CIFR's 23.5 EH/s 2025 target was suspended without formal withdrawal as Black Pearl was repurposed for HPC. Both companies are transitioning away from mining without declaring it — the transition shows in capital allocation, not in stated strategy.

### Unverified Claims (Single-Source, Counterparty Absent)

**1. IREN's Sweetwater site interest ("largest companies in the world," "advanced stages").**
Six quarters of claims, no named counterparty ever disclosed, Morgan Stanley process terminated. The counterparty is never cited independently. This is the clearest unverified claim in the dataset.

**2. CIFR's Stingray "preferred tenant" relationship.**
Page used "I would not be speaking so confidently if I were not so confident" in Q4 2025. Unsigned through Q1 2026. No counterparty named. One company's claim, no corroboration.

**3. IREN's $3.4B ARR target including ~$1B uncontracted.**
No counterparty named for the 40,000 additional GPUs. No corroboration from any disclosed customer.

**4. CIFR's claim of "officially arriving as a leader" in HPC (Q3 2025).**
Stated before a dollar of HPC revenue had been recognized. Not corroborated by market position data, customer citations, or competitive benchmarks.

**The uniformity is the signal.** These are not the same companies using the same PR firm. Both claims are unfalsifiable by design — they cannot be contradicted by any observable outcome short of disclosed signed contracts, which remain withheld.

**Sector FOG Index: 7/10 (High).** The sector's prepared remark language is systematically less informative than Q&A in both companies. Q&A produces the useful data; prepared remarks produce the narrative. This is a structural pattern, not quarterly variation.

## 6. Aggregated Flags & Risks

### Risks Appearing Across Both Companies

**1. Perishable goal replacement (highest priority).**
Both companies exhibit the same pattern: aspirational milestones that expire without acknowledgment and are replaced by new aspirational milestones at a longer horizon. IREN's Sweetwater claim has reset every quarter for six consecutive quarters. CIFR's Barber Lake tenant claim has reset every quarter since Q1 2025. Neither company has formally withdrawn a prior target — they simply add new, later-dated aspirations. This is the single most consistent communication risk in the combined dataset.

**2. AI pipeline conflation with signed anchors.**
Both companies present signed anchor contracts and unsigned pipeline with co-equal confidence. IREN's $3.4B ARR target treats a $1B uncontracted component as equivalent to the $2.3B contracted. CIFR's 3.2 GW pipeline treats 600 MW of signed capacity and 2,600 MW of unsigned capacity as a unified pipeline number. This creates systematic forecast inflation.

**3. Equity erosion during high capital deployment.**
IREN: equity declined from $2,876M (Q3 FY2025) to $2,511M (Q4 FY2025) — a $365M single-quarter reduction with no management explanation. CIFR: total equity declined from $805.5M (Q4 2024) to $783.2M (Q3 2025) despite a $1.3B convertible note offering in Q3 2025. In both cases, equity is shrinking during a period of declared growth and capital raises. Neither management team has addressed this dynamic in prepared remarks.

**4. Financing opacity at maximum deployment.**
CIFR has deferred disclosure of construction financing terms for both the AWS and Fluidstack/Google builds across two consecutive calls ("not prepared to give specifics," "will update in short order"). IREN has not disclosed the cost-of-capital framework for its dilutive ATM program ($823M through FY2024; $9.2B total capital raises by Q2 FY2026). Both companies are deploying multi-billion dollar capital programs with incomplete financing disclosure.

**5. CEO prepared remarks > Q&A quality gap.**
Both companies show an identical pattern: CEO prepared remarks carry high FOG density; CEO Q&A responses are more specific. This is documented explicitly for IREN (nine quarters) and CIFR (entire series). It suggests management is *capable* of precision but *chooses* promotion when scripted.

### Risks Isolated to Single Companies

**IREN only:**
- Co-CEO Will Roberts entirely absent from Q2 FY2026 call — unacknowledged.
- Microsoft prepayment cash inflating balance sheet optics ($2.8B cash at Q2 FY2026 includes prepayments that are liabilities against future GPU delivery).
- Q2 FY2026 revenue decline of 23% sequentially received no candid treatment in prepared remarks.
- Transcription errors left uncorrected on calls ($1M vs. $1B convertible; June 2023 maturity date for an active note).

**CIFR only:**
- CFO succession risk: Ed Farrell's departure removed the most consistently specific voice in the series; Greg Mumford's first prepared remarks are described as substantially boilerplate.
- Q4 2025 "business update" format: essentially no financial data provided during active multi-hundred-megawatt construction. The lowest-graded call in the combined dataset.
- SoftBank exclusivity lapse disclosed only under analyst pressure in Q1 2025 — material information withheld from prepared remarks.
- Non-GAAP gap widening: five simultaneous adjustments in Q4 2024 without call-level explanation. GAAP-to-non-GAAP spread is the widest documented in this dataset.

## 7. Capital Deployment Aggregation

### Per-Company Financials (Financial Metrics Table — Totals Across All Reported Quarters)

| Metric | CIFR | IREN | Sector Total |
|--------|------|------|-------------|
| Revenue | $131M | $873M | **$1,004M** |
| CapEx | –$275M | –$2,107M | **–$2,382M** |
| FCF | –$380M | –$1,643M | **–$2,023M** |
| CapEx / Revenue ratio | 2.1× | 2.4× | **2.4×** |
| FCF deficit vs. Revenue | 2.9× | 1.9× | **2.0×** |

*Note: CIFR data covers 13 quarters; IREN data covers 10 quarters. Periods are not fully co-terminus. Comparison is directional, not quarter-matched.*

### Key Observations

**1. The sector is burning cash at twice its revenue rate.** The combined FCF deficit of –$2,023M against $1,004M in revenue means that for every dollar of revenue recognized, approximately two dollars of cash has been consumed. This is pre-revenue infrastructure logic — acceptable if the signed contracts convert to durable cash flows — but it is the baseline against which hyperscaler contract announcements must be evaluated.

**2. IREN dominates sector capital deployment.** IREN accounts for 88% of combined CapEx ($2,107M of $2,382M) and 87% of combined revenue ($873M of $1,004M). The Microsoft contract and $3.6B GPU financing facility will expand this disproportion materially in future quarters. IREN is the dominant capital deployer in this two-company dataset; CIFR is a substantially smaller player by deployed capital.

**3. CIFR's CapEx is accelerating sharply at series end.** CapEx went from –$45.1M in Q3 2025 to –$230.1M in Q4 2025 — a 5× sequential increase corresponding to the AWS and Fluidstack/Google construction commitments. This is the steepest single-quarter CapEx acceleration in the combined dataset and precedes any disclosed construction financing terms.

**4. The Gerstner Check — does the bottom-up sum challenge sector narratives?**
Sector commentary frequently frames these companies as beneficiaries of a multi-trillion-dollar AI infrastructure wave. The combined disclosed revenue is $1.0B over the full periods analyzed. The combined signed forward contract value (Microsoft + AWS + Fluidstack/Google) is approximately $18.2B in lifetime value. The gap between current revenue run rate and implied forward revenue is real — but it is entirely dependent on contract execution, counterparty performance, and construction delivery on schedule. The $18.2B in signed contracts does not appear in historical financial metrics; it is future contingent value. Anyone pricing these companies on contract value rather than current cash generation is underwriting execution risk that neither management team has yet demonstrated the ability to deliver at this scale.

**5. Cash positions are misleading without liability context.**
IREN reported $2,800M in cash at Q2 FY2026 — but this figure includes Microsoft prepayments that are effectively contractual liabilities (GPU delivery obligations). CIFR reported $1,207M in cash at Q3 2025 — but $1.3B of that is from 0% coupon convertibles that will eventually require equity dilution or repayment. Gross cash figures in both companies overstate freely deployable capital.

### Priority Accountability Items for Next Reporting Cycle

Based on the combined analysis, the three highest-priority verification items across both companies:

1. **CIFR Stingray lease:** If unsigned at Q2 2026, this is the single most consequential unresolved commitment in the dataset. Tyler Page's "I would not be speaking so confidently" language from Q4 2025 makes this the clearest credibility test.

2. **IREN Sweetwater monetization:** Six consecutive quarters of "advanced conversations." A seventh without a named counterparty confirms that this is a recurring narrative placeholder, not a near-term transaction.

3. **CIFR construction financing terms:** Two consecutive deferrals on billion-dollar obligations. A third deferral at Q2 2026 would be the most significant financial transparency failure in the combined series.
