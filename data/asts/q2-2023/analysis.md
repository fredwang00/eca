# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: AST SpaceMobile (ASTS) | Quarter: Q2 2023 | Date: August 13, 2023

---

### Executive Summary

AST SpaceMobile's Q2 2023 call presents a company at an early but genuinely consequential inflection point—real technical milestones have been achieved, and the financial picture is disclosed with reasonable specificity given the company's pre-revenue stage. However, the call is materially weakened by vague commercial framing (40 MOUs covering 2.4 billion subscribers is cited repeatedly with no conversion visibility), a strategic capital raise described in terms so hedged as to provide almost no actionable information, and a Q&A section where several direct investor questions received answers that circled the topic rather than landing on it. A thoughtful long-term investor would come away confident in the engineering progress, uncertain about the commercial runway, and frustrated by the opacity surrounding the financing that the company is treating as its central near-term event.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: B**

Sean Wallace provided more financial specificity than is typical for a pre-revenue space technology company, and that deserves genuine credit. Non-GAAP adjusted cash operating expenses were cited as $38.4 million in Q2 versus $40.3 million in Q1, with a specific forward outlook ("mid- to high 30s for at least 2 more quarters"). CapEx was given with precision ($12.1 million in Q2 vs. $14.4 million in Q1) along with a forward range ($15–$20 million per quarter in H2), and the $45–$50 million launch services payment was identified as a discrete Q3 event—exactly the kind of milestone-specific disclosure that helps investors model cash consumption. Cash on hand was stated as "just shy of $192 million," and the $179 million financing package was itemized usefully: $64 million in equity, a $15 million equipment loan, and a $48.5 million draw on a $100 million senior secured facility with specific borrower identified (Lone Star Bank).

The grade falls short of an A for two reasons. First, the non-GAAP adjustments—particularly the $19.6 million in noncash expenses driven by BlueWalker 3 amortization—are disclosed but not contextualized in terms of what they imply for the satellite's book life, impairment risk, or relationship to commercial timelines. Second, the "going concern" language from the 10-Q ("sufficient to support our expenditures for at least the next 12 months") is acknowledged but not stress-tested: no scenario analysis is offered for what happens if the strategic capital raise closes late or not at all, which is a material omission given that Wallace himself noted the outcome "cannot be guaranteed."

---

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: C**

The core strategic thesis—build the world's first space-based cellular broadband network operating directly with existing smartphones, capture a share of the 5-billion-phone market via MNO partnerships—is stated clearly and repeated consistently across speakers. That is a genuine positive. The company also demonstrates quarter-over-quarter milestone tracking: the Q1 2024 launch target for 5 BlueBird satellites is reaffirmed, and the 3-month post-launch calibration estimate is a specific, if hedged, commitment ("it may take longer, but that's what we're estimating").

However, the commercial layer of the strategy is almost entirely aspirational. The phrase "over 40 MOUs and agreements with MNOs globally that have around 2.4 billion subscribers" has appeared in consecutive quarterly calls without meaningful evolution—no MOUs disclosed by name (other than AT&T and Vodafone, who are testing partners), no revenue-sharing terms described, no conversion of MOUs to binding agreements noted. When Benjamin Soff asks directly about whether Block 1 satellites can generate free cash flow, Scott Wisniewski's answer—"interesting opportunities," "not cash flow positive"—is candid in one dimension but strategically vague in every other. What applications? What pricing? What MNOs? The company has a coherent engineering strategy and a placeholder commercial strategy, and the call does not bridge the two.

---

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: C**

The call is predominantly investor-facing, which is expected for an earnings call, but the imbalance is notable. Employees are mentioned once, by Sean Wallace: "We could not be able to define this new industry without our hard-working team of engineers, technicians and suppliers." This is a generic platitude—no team names, no specific engineering achievements attributed to individuals or groups, no acknowledgment of the difficulty of what has been built. The Midland, Texas workforce, who presumably executed the manufacturing readiness review Abel references, receives no specific recognition despite being central to the company's vertically integrated model.

Customer-side thinking is almost entirely absent. The 5 billion potential users are invoked as a market size metric, not as people with real connectivity needs the company is trying to serve. The Hyderabad R&D center announcement gestures toward the Indian market but frames it entirely in terms of "access to an incredible engineering talent" and market size—not in terms of what connectivity would mean to Indian users or what the commercial structure with Indian MNOs would look like. Partner MNOs are mentioned warmly but generically ("some of the most sophisticated wireless companies in the world"), with AT&T and Vodafone cited primarily as validators of technical milestones rather than as commercial partners with specific terms or commitments disclosed.

---

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: C**

The prepared remarks are riddled with recurring FOG that is distinct from—and partially obscures—the real substance the call contains. Abel Avellan in particular relies on a small vocabulary of superlatives and self-referential milestone claims that become formulaic across the call. The Q&A is better than the prepared remarks, but several analyst questions received answers that were substantively incomplete or redirected.

**Specific FOG examples:**

1. *"We have made history again"* — used twice in Abel's prepared remarks within three paragraphs. A historic achievement can be stated once with evidence. Repeated self-description as history-making without letting the audience assess the claim is an authority assertion rather than a demonstration.

2. *"The markets we are pursuing are massive, and we have a significant first mover advantage"* (Scott Wisniewski, prepared remarks) — both claims are asserted without supporting evidence in the sentence or adjacent sentences. First mover advantage in what, specifically, and against whom?

3. *"We are proud to have established our footprint with such a great partners"* — grammatically awkward and substantively empty. "Footprint" and "great partners" convey nothing measurable.

4. *"Integrating the ubiquitous coverage of a satellite constellation into the terrestrial wireless infrastructure will be one of the most exciting innovations in communications in the last 25 years"* (Sean Wallace) — this is a promotional claim dressed as analysis. It is also unverifiable. FOG.

5. On the strategic capital raise, when analysts would most benefit from specificity, both Abel and Sean retreat into procedural hedging: *"The size, complexity and commercial and strategic nature of these negotiations require significant time to complete."* This sentence describes why they cannot be specific but does not provide any substitute information. Compare with what could have been said: which verticals the strategic partners operate in, approximate scale of expected commitment, timeline for announcement.

In Q&A, Abel's response to Benjamin Soff's question about whether Block 1 satellites can fund the business includes: *"the completion of BlueWalker 3 testing, the lean budget confirmations, the number of types of devices that we're able to interoperate with our technology, that confirms basically all the fundamentals of our business plan."* The question was whether those 5 satellites could generate free cash flow on their own. This answer confirms the business plan is intact—but does not answer the question asked.

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: B**

Abel Avellan's vision is more specific than most pre-revenue CEOs offer, and it is to his credit that it is falsifiable: 90 satellites for global broadband coverage, 6 satellites per month manufacturing capacity as a target, Q1 2024 launch window, 3-month orbital calibration estimate. These are specific enough that investors can hold the company accountable to them. The "90 satellites for global broadband" figure is the kind of concrete, structural claim that distinguishes a real technology plan from a pitch deck. Similarly, the manufacturing facility capacity claim (6 satellites per month with 185,000 sq ft) is verifiable and specific.

The call also demonstrates genuine long-term orientation in the manufacturing buildout—the vertically integrated strategy (starting from aluminum and composite materials) reflects a deliberate decision to trade short-term capital efficiency for supply chain control and cost structure advantages at scale. Avellan explains this trade-off explicitly in response to Chris Quilty's supply chain question, which is good investor education.

What prevents an A is the absence of any "What Went Wrong" reporting. BlueWalker 3 took significantly longer and was more expensive than originally projected. The company's stock has been under sustained pressure. Neither of these realities is addressed with candor or ownership. Avellan says "We are very, very happy with everything that we had accomplished with BlueWalker 3"—which may be true from a technical standpoint—but a long-term-oriented CEO would also acknowledge where the program overran, what was learned from that overrun, and how it has been incorporated into the Block 1 program's planning assumptions. That acknowledgment is absent.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **The strategic capital raise disclosure is a trust-building failure in progress.** The company has described this raise as central to funding operations through 2024–2025, yet it cannot name parties, disclose structure, indicate size, or provide a timing range—beyond "requires significant time." This creates an information vacuum that the market will fill with speculation. When the deal eventually closes (or fails to close), investors will reassess whether the interim communications were candid or merely reassuring. The pattern of saying "multiple indications of interest from several parties" without any corroborating specificity is a pattern that erodes credibility over time.

- **The MOU count has become a lagging metric.** "Over 40 MOUs and agreements... around 2.4 billion subscribers" appears to be carried forward from prior calls without evolution. Investors cannot assess whether these are deepening into binding agreements, generating co-development revenue, or remaining perpetually at the letter-of-intent stage. A count that does not change—or changes only upward in aggregate without qualitative disclosure about the state of individual relationships—eventually reads as marketing rather than progress.

- **No acknowledgment of schedule or cost slippage history.** The company's original commercial timelines have slipped materially. Not addressing this creates a gap between management's self-description ("very, very happy with everything") and the investor's lived experience of the stock. CEOs who fail to acknowledge what went wrong in prior periods lose credibility on forward guidance—even when that guidance is well-reasoned.

- **CFO language around going-concern adjacent risk is understated.** Wallace's statement that cash is "sufficient to support expenditures for at least the next 12 months" is standard 10-Q language, but its insertion in the earnings call—combined with the undisclosed strategic raise and the explicit statement that "we cannot guarantee that any agreement will be reached"—creates a risk signal that is not matched by the call's generally optimistic tone. The mismatch between tone and disclosed risk is itself a communication risk.

---

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **Financial specificity from the CFO is genuinely above average for a pre-revenue space company.** The itemization of the financing package ($64M equity, $15M equipment loan, $48.5M draw on $100M facility), the forward CapEx guidance ($15–$20M/quarter in H2), and the discrete identification of the $45–$50M launch services payment in Q3 are all specific enough to build a basic cash model. This is not obvious or universal in this sector.

- **Technical milestones are described with appropriate precision and named counterparties.** The 4G LTE test yielding speeds "above 10 megabits per second" with AT&T, Vodafone, and Nokia named is a high-candor disclosure. Naming counterparties converts an assertion into a verifiable claim and signals that the company is not managing impressions.

- **The 90-satellite figure for global coverage is a falsifiable, specific strategic anchor.** In a sector where constellation size is often described vaguely, Avellan's crisp statement that "our number of total satellites needs for having global broadband connectivity is around 90 satellites" is useful to investors and distinguishes ASTS's capital requirements from larger constellation competitors in a concrete way.

- **Abel Avellan's response to the Samsung SOS question shows product discipline.** Rather than pivoting to endorse Samsung's feature as an adjacent opportunity, Avellan stayed focused on broadband as the primary value proposition: "if you can do 10 megabits, you can do SOS, you can do voice." This is a tight, principled answer that resists the temptation to expand the TAM narrative in ways that could dilute the core strategy.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

*(This appears to be the first evaluation in this framework for AST SpaceMobile.)*

**Commitments and claims to verify in Q3 2023 / next earnings call:**

1. **Q1 2024 BlueBird launch window:** Avellan affirmed this target twice. Any slip—or any change in language from "Q1 2024" to "first half of 2024"—is a material accountability signal. Note also that "several coming milestones with SpaceX" were cited as the mechanism for narrowing the launch window; those milestones should be publicly trackable or at minimum referenced in the next call.

2. **$45–$50 million launch services payment in Q3:** Wallace stated explicitly this would be paid in Q3 2023. Verification is straightforward: cash on hand at end of Q3 should reflect this outflow. If the payment is deferred without explanation, that is a negative signal.

3. **Strategic capital raise resolution:** The company has described multiple nonbinding indications of interest from unnamed parties. By the next call, either a deal will have closed, negotiations will be ongoing, or the process will have failed. Any of these outcomes carries disclosure implications. Watch for: whether the same "multiple indications of interest" language is recycled, whether counterparties remain unnamed, and whether the company discloses deal structure (debt vs. equity ratio, valuation basis).

4. **MOU count evolution:** Currently stated as "over 40 MOUs and agreements... around 2.4 billion subscribers." If this figure does not evolve in specificity—i.e., if no MOUs are converted to commercial agreements with disclosed terms—by the next call, flag as a commercial accountability concern.

5. **Non-GAAP cash operating expenses guidance:** Wallace projected "mid- to high 30s for at least 2 more quarters." Q3 2023 actuals will either confirm or refute this guidance, providing a direct test of CFO forecast reliability.
