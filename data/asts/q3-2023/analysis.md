# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: AST SpaceMobile | Quarter: Q3 2023 | Date: November 13, 2023

---

### Executive Summary

AST SpaceMobile's Q3 2023 call is the communication of a pre-revenue, capital-intensive technology company that has genuinely accomplished something technically impressive—but whose executives have not yet learned to communicate with the discipline that the company's financial precarity demands. The call mixes legitimate, specific technical and operational disclosures with persistent evasion on capital formation (the most consequential near-term question), and the Q&A format—driven largely by pre-screened shareholder questions rather than adversarial analyst probing—obscures how little hard information was actually provided. A thoughtful long-term investor would leave this call confident in the engineering team and deeply uncertain about whether the company will be adequately funded to deploy it.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: C**

The CFO, Sean Wallace, provides several specific and useful data points: non-GAAP adjusted cash operating expenses of $37.3 million vs. $38.4 million in Q2; Q3 CapEx of $71.7 million vs. $26.8 million in Q2; cash on hand of approximately $135 million; and a revised BB1-1 cost estimate of $115 million, up from $110 million—with the reason for the increase disclosed (an orbital inclination change requested by a potential customer). The forward guidance on opex reduction ($25M–$30M per quarter beginning Q1 2024, down from $37M–$40M) is quantified and attributed to specific drivers (completion of third-party R&D milestones, ASIC design work). These are genuine credits.

However, the disclosure has a critical structural hole: the company's most material financial question—the strategic investment process that is meant to fund the constellation beyond the first five satellites—is addressed almost entirely in aspirational language. Abel Avellan states only that the process is "moving forward on definitive documentation and completion of diligence with multiple strategic partners" and that he hopes "to be able to share more at any time during November or December." No terms, no structure, no counterparty names, no timeline beyond a two-month window. Given that the company had $135 million in cash and had spent 85% of BB1-1 CapEx, the funding question is existential—and the disclosure is near-zero. The CFO's characterization of liquidity—"sufficient to support our expenditures for at least the next 12 months"—is the minimum legal disclosure threshold, not investor-grade communication. The absence of any quantification of the capital needed to fund the full constellation (beyond the first five satellites) leaves investors unable to assess dilution risk, timeline risk, or commercial viability.

---

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: B-**

The core strategy is articulate and specific enough to be summarized: build the only large-phased-array satellite constellation capable of delivering broadband directly to unmodified smartphones, manufacture at vertical integration and low cost in Midland, partner with MNOs who own the subscriber relationships, and monetize through those relationships. This is genuinely differentiated from generic tech strategy, and Avellan communicates it with evident conviction. The technology differentiation argument—broadband vs. narrowband, 5G vs. SMS/emergency SOS, unmodified phone vs. special chipset—is made specifically and is falsifiable. The Qualcomm/Iridium commentary ("the moment that you do that, you take that 5 billion phones in circulation to a niche play") is one of the sharper competitive analysis moments in the call.

The accountability gaps are meaningful, however. The company's prior guidance—Q1 2024 launch of first five satellites—is restated without revisiting whether anything has changed. The CapEx estimate revision ($110M to $115M) is disclosed but not flagged as a miss against prior guidance; it is reframed as a positive (customer-driven inclination change). More significantly, the MNO relationship language—"over 40 of the largest mobile network operators," "over 2 billion subscribers"—has appeared in prior materials and is not advanced. No MOU has converted to a signed commercial agreement; this is acknowledged but not dwelt upon. The call does not revisit any specific commitment from the Q2 call to assess what was promised vs. delivered.

---

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: C**

The call is overwhelmingly investor-facing. Customers—the eventual subscribers who would use the service—are described almost exclusively as an aggregate addressable market ("5 billion phones in circulation," "billions of people without cellular broadband") rather than as human beings with specific needs. Employee recognition is present but entirely generic: Wallace says he wants to "recognize and thank the hard-working team of engineers, technicians, suppliers," which is boilerplate indistinguishable from any manufacturing company's earnings call. The one humanizing detail—"over 100 of them in the picture above"—refers to a slide the call's audio audience cannot see.

MNO partners receive the most substantive treatment, though still primarily as validation signals rather than as relationships with texture. Citing AT&T's and Vodafone's public endorsements is legitimate credibility-building, but the actual terms of collaboration, revenue-sharing expectations, or the partners' specific use-case commitments are entirely absent. Avellan's authentic enthusiasm for the technology ("we invented direct-to-device," participation in presidential spectrum strategy) comes through as genuine, not scripted, which is a mild positive signal for culture. But there is no evidence of an ownership culture in the financial communication—no discussion of capital allocation trade-offs, no acknowledgment of the tension between shareholder dilution risk and constellation funding needs.

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: C**

The call contains a meaningful ratio of substantive technical disclosure—specific numbers, component-level manufacturing detail, test results with quantified data rates—that keeps it from scoring D. But the prepared remarks and Q&A are riddled with FOG, particularly on the topics that matter most to investors.

**Specific FOG examples:**

1. *"Fundraising efforts with multiple strategic partners continue to advance and progress and the timing is in line with what we disclosed to you a few weeks ago."* — "Advance and progress" is redundant and says nothing. "In line with what we disclosed" defers substance to a prior disclosure that itself contained no specifics.

2. *"Overall, we're encouraged by where we are in this process."* — Pure sentiment without information. Repeated in Q&A: "We're very encouraged where we are right now." The word "encouraged" appears as a content-free placeholder throughout.

3. *"We are now giving and defining the satellite-to-device industry, which is rapidly becoming one of the most exciting innovations in the mobile phone industry in the last 50 years."* — Unsupported superlative. No evidence is provided for the "last 50 years" claim, and "giving and defining" is grammatically incoherent.

4. *"There have been a tremendous in about direct-to-device services. Over the past year, we have seen other companies pull their hands up on a me-too offering."* — Garbled syntax aside, "me-too offering" is a dismissive competitive characterization offered without specific evidence about named competitors' technical limitations.

5. *"So yes, that caught our eyes. We're very happy. We're very encouraged about that."* — Three consecutive content-free sentiment statements in response to a specific policy question about spectrum regulation. The response eventually produces one real claim (that the presidential strategy "replicates what we have invented") but buries it in filler.

The Q&A structure is a further FOG risk: the call begins with pre-screened shareholder questions before opening to analysts. This format is unusual and tilts the disclosure environment toward softballs. The analyst questions that do arrive are handled with reasonable directness by Avellan (the ASIC and competitive landscape responses are specific), but the strategic investment question—the most important topic—receives a one-paragraph deflection.

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: B-**

Avellan's vision is more specific than most pre-revenue tech CEOs manage. The target is falsifiable: broadband (not narrowband), 5G-capable, directly to unmodified phones, via the existing spectrum licensed to MNO partners. The ASIC roadmap—10x improvement in processing bandwidth from 1 GHz to 10 GHz, 3–4 months post-tape-out delivery from TSMC—is specific and verifiable. The manufacturing capacity claim (six satellites per month, 72 per year) is concrete and tied to describable infrastructure. The competitive thesis is argued rather than simply asserted: the Qualcomm/Iridium departure is interpreted through a coherent lens (special chipset = niche market) rather than dismissed.

Long-term orientation is evident in the four-year, $40M ASIC investment and the vertical integration strategy, which sacrificed short-term simplicity (outsourcing) for long-term cost control. The disclosure of what went wrong with BlueWalker 3 testing (having to move the satellite off-site multiple times, including a late trip to California) and the specific remedy (in-house testing capability at Site Two in Midland) is a genuine "What Went Wrong" moment handled honestly.

What prevents a higher grade: no falsifiable revenue or subscriber targets are offered; the vision contains no discussion of how revenue will actually accrue (which MNOs will be first, at what rates, under what contractual structure); and the call contains no investor education on the key metrics a sophisticated investor should track to assess whether the strategy is working. "Over 40 MNOs" and "over 2 billion subscribers" are stated as milestones, but the gap between MOU and commercial contract—which is the actual value-creation hurdle—is not discussed.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **The strategic investment disclosure is a black box at a moment of existential financial consequence.** With $135M in cash, 85% of BB1-1 CapEx spent, and an entire constellation to fund, the management team's complete inability to describe the structure, scale, or counterparty type of the strategic investment process is not appropriate disclosure humility—it is information asymmetry that disadvantages all public shareholders. The pattern of using "encouraged" as a substitute for information should be flagged as a recurring deflection tactic.

- **The MOU-to-contract conversion gap is unaddressed and aging.** The company has cited "40+ MNOs" and "2 billion subscribers" across multiple quarters without any commercial agreement converting to a signed revenue contract. The call does not acknowledge this gap, nor does it explain what conditions must be met for conversion. Each quarter this metric is restated without progress on conversion, its credibility as a leading indicator declines.

- **The Q&A format is structurally tilted toward favorable questions.** Opening with pre-screened shareholder questions before analyst questions is a non-standard format that reduces the adversarial probing that reveals real-time candor. Only three analysts appear to have asked questions, and the most probing one (Griffin Boss on concurrent call capacity testing) received a qualitative answer ("we have done multiple and simultaneous") where quantitative data was clearly available and would have been more useful.

- **CapEx estimate revision is framed as a positive.** The $5M increase in BB1-1 cost ($110M to $115M) is described as "a good portion of the increase in cost was a result of a potential customer requesting a change in inclination." This is a legitimate explanation, but it converts a cost overrun into a demand signal without acknowledging that the estimate changed. The CFO should have stated the prior guidance, the variance, and the reason—rather than leading with the positive interpretation.

---

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **Technical specificity in manufacturing disclosure is genuinely high.** The description of vertically integrated subsystems—solar panel production, battery pack lines, FPGA-based electronics, in-house beamformers, ControlSat avionics—is unusually concrete for an earnings call and gives investors real insight into the operational state of the business. The claim that 85% of planned CapEx is spent is specific and verifiable against subsequent filings.

- **The ASIC roadmap is falsifiable.** "First units approximately 3–4 months after tape-out," "10x improvement in processing bandwidth from 1 GHz to 10 GHz," "$40M investment over four years"—these are checkable claims with enough specificity to hold management accountable. This is the strongest disclosure moment in the call.

- **The BlueWalker 3 testing-inefficiency disclosure and remedy is a candor positive.** Acknowledging that prior satellite testing required multiple off-site trips (including a last-minute return to California), and then explaining the specific infrastructure investment made to prevent recurrence (in-house thermal, vibration, and RF testing at Site Two), follows the "What Went Wrong and What We Did About It" pattern that Rittenhouse identifies as a trust-building signal.

- **The competitive differentiation argument is reasoned rather than asserted.** Avellan's response to the Qualcomm/Iridium question is one of the more intellectually honest competitive analyses heard on pre-revenue tech calls: he identifies a structural logic (proprietary chipset requirement reduces addressable market from 5 billion to a niche), applies it to the competitor's exit, and uses it to reinforce rather than merely assert his own positioning. This is a demonstration of dualistic thinking—holding a clear view of why competitors fail while being specific about what his own system must do to succeed.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

**This is the first evaluation of this company under this framework. The following specific commitments and claims should be verified in Q4 2023 and Q1 2024:**

1. **Strategic investment announcement "during November or December" (2023).** Avellan stated: "I hope to be able to share more at any time during November or December." If Q4 2024 arrives without a disclosed strategic investment—with named counterparties, structure, and scale—this should be scored as a material accountability failure, not merely a timing slip.

2. **First five commercial satellites (BB1-1) launch in Q1 2024.** This is the primary operational commitment of the call. Any delay should be disclosed with specific cause and revised timeline, not managed with vague language about "continued progress."

3. **Adjusted operating expenses of $25M–$30M per quarter beginning Q1 2024.** The CFO provided specific guidance. Next quarter's non-GAAP adjusted cash opex should be checked against this range, and any variance should trigger inquiry into whether the described completion of third-party R&D milestones actually occurred.

4. **ASIC tape-out timing and first-unit delivery.** The design was described as "substantially complete," with first units expected 3–4 months post-tape-out. The tape-out date, when disclosed, starts the clock on this verifiable commitment.

5. **MOU-to-commercial agreement conversion.** The company should be asked directly on the Q4 call: how many of the 40+ MNOs have converted from MOU to signed commercial agreements with economic terms, and what are the conditions precedent for conversion? If the number remains zero through launch, it is a significant commercial execution risk that the communication has systematically underemphasized.
