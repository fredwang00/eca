# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: AST SpaceMobile (ASTS) | Quarter: Q1 2023 | Date: May 14, 2023

---

### Executive Summary

AST SpaceMobile's Q1 2023 call communicates a genuine technical milestone—the first direct voice call from a satellite to an unmodified smartphone—with reasonable specificity in the engineering and financial dimensions, while relying heavily on aspirational language and self-congratulatory framing in strategic and cultural commentary. The CFO (Sean Wallace) provides the most substantive and credible disclosure of the three speakers; Abel Avellan's remarks combine real technical depth with a pattern of unsupported superlatives and avoided specifics on the questions that matter most to long-term investors (revenue timeline, financing certainty, competitive validation). A thoughtful long-term investor would leave this call encouraged by the technology proof-point but appropriately cautious about the company's ability to bridge its capital gap to commercial service.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: B**

Sean Wallace provides notably specific financial detail for a pre-revenue company: non-GAAP adjusted operating expenses of $40.3 million (vs. $39.1 million in Q4 2022), capex of $13 million (vs. $10.4 million), a specific forward range of $100–$110 million for Block 1 satellite costs, and $40 million already expended against that figure. The cash position is disclosed precisely ("just shy of $185.7 million") and the investment policy is explained with counterparty specificity (JPMorgan Asset Management). The decision to begin depreciating BlueWalker 3 over 16 months, with the resulting $17 million+ per quarter in non-cash charges, is disclosed proactively—a meaningful candor credit, as many pre-revenue companies would have buried this implication. However, the capital raising section contains language that warrants scrutiny: "we are encouraged by these initial efforts" on debt and strategic financing is immediately hedged with "there can be no assurance that additional funds will be available to us on favorable terms or at all"—which is legally required but also accurately describes an uncertain situation the company does not elaborate on further. The score drops from A because there is no explicit link between current burn rate and runway beyond the 12-month minimum disclosure threshold, no quantified revenue model tied to the five Block 1 satellites, and no discussion of what happens if commercial service is delayed past Q2 2024.

---

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: B-/C+** (scored as C for weighting purposes)

The core strategic proposition is intelligible and consistently stated: build the first satellite network delivering cellular broadband directly to unmodified phones, using carrier spectrum, at scale. This is a legitimate differentiator and Avellan articulates the technical logic behind it with more precision than most CEOs ("three bits per hertz, sufficient to support 4G and 5G data rate speeds"). The technology roadmap is staged and quantified in relative terms: BlueWalker 3 establishes baseline, Block 1 delivers 10x capacity per satellite, Block 2 delivers another 10x—a 100x improvement over the test satellite. This is a defensible and clear framework. However, the call fails to revisit any forward-looking commitments made in prior periods. There is no "last quarter we said X; here is where we stand" structure. The "~40 MNOs under preliminary agreements" figure has appeared in prior updates without any disclosed progress toward binding commercial agreements—this is a stale metric being recycled as forward momentum. When Caleb Henry asks directly about capacity guidance ("more than 9 to 13 gigabits per satellite?"), Avellan declines: "We are not providing guidance, but we are still within the range that we have provided before"—a non-answer that protects the company from accountability. The strategic milestones are directional (Q1 2024 launch, 3–4 month activation period) but revenue timing is never discussed.

---

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: C**

The call is investor-facing almost exclusively. Customers—the billions of unconnected users whose lives would ostensibly change—appear only as a market-size statistic ("over 5 billion mobile devices in circulation") and never as real people with described needs. There is a brief and genuine nod to the engineering team: "I want to congratulate our team on this groundbreaking milestone"—but this is boilerplate recognition, not the specific acknowledgment that would signal genuine ownership culture. The capital stewardship section does include one authentic and meaningful signal: Abel Avellan "does not draw a salary" and "remains the largest shareholder," with returns tied to company performance. This is the kind of specific, verifiable alignment disclosure that builds trust and is credit-worthy. MNO partners (AT&T, Vodafone) are mentioned with appropriate executive-level name-dropping (Margherita the Vodafone CEO, Chris at AT&T Networks) that suggests real relationships. However, the community of users who would benefit from connectivity is entirely abstracted; there is no mention of how employees are navigating a financially constrained pre-revenue environment; and supply chain partners (component manufacturers, launch providers) are referenced only in the context of AST's own execution.

---

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: C**

The prepared remarks suffer from a persistent elevation of the ordinary into the historic. The call opens with the word "history" or "historic" used four times in the first three minutes and continues at similar frequency throughout. Many of Avellan's prepared remarks substitute rhetorical intensity for substantive disclosure. The Q&A reveals a different register: Avellan is technically precise when answering engineering questions (beam pointing accuracy "within one, one and a half degrees to two and a half degrees depending on the size of the array"), which makes the FOG in the prepared remarks more conspicuous by contrast—it appears to be a deliberate presentation layer, not a reflection of imprecise thinking.

**Specific FOG examples:**

1. *"This is truly unbelievable."* — Used without irony to describe AT&T's historical comparison of the voice call. Self-congratulation presented as endorsement.

2. *"This is truly a game changer in a market we believe we have a very significant first mover advantage."* — Two clichés stacked with an unsupported superlative. The first-mover advantage claim may be accurate but is asserted, not substantiated, in this passage.

3. *"Some of these relationships are deep with a strong executive support. They are truly in the room with us helping to bring this technology to reality."* — "Truly in the room" is atmospheric language masquerading as a disclosure. Which executives? Doing what specifically?

4. *"We continue strong momentum with the wireless industry."* — "Strong momentum" is a weasel phrase that conveys directional optimism without any verifiable content.

5. *"We are encouraged by these initial efforts"* (regarding capital raising) — This is the one FOG instance that carries real risk: it creates an impression of progress on financing while the accompanying hedge ("no assurance that additional funds will be available") signals material uncertainty. The encouragement is offered with nothing behind it.

The Q&A is largely responsive and technically direct. The only notable Q&A evasion is the capacity guidance decline, which is standard practice but worth noting.

---

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: B**

Avellan demonstrates genuine long-term thinking in at least two ways. First, the technology roadmap is staged and explicitly connected to unit economics: "we are not only improving our unitary cost, we continue to improve our technology, but we're also improving our capacity and our network capacity as we add more satellites." This is a real causal chain, not aspiration. Second, the discussion of vertical integration—owning "all the software, all the hardware, all the custom ASICs"—is linked credibly to cost structure and capacity improvement, not merely recited as a differentiator. The constellation size revision (from 110 to 95 satellites) is disclosed and explained as a positive revision based on updated signal-strength-by-elevation data—this is the kind of honest mid-course adjustment that builds credibility. However, the vision statement itself is never made falsifiable. There is no target for subscriber penetration, no revenue per satellite estimate, no timeline to operating cash flow beyond "before completing our constellation." Wallace's remark that "the modularity of our business plan creates the potential to reach operating cash flow with a modest portion of our planned constellation" is a meaningful structural claim but is not supported with any numbers. The absence of any "what went wrong" reporting—no discussion of what was harder than expected with BlueWalker 3, what caused any delays, what the ASIC timeline slippage means—limits the grade. Companies that disclose only the positive half of the ledger train investors to discount all of their disclosures over time.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **The "40 MNOs" number is becoming a perishable metric.** It has appeared across multiple quarters without disclosed progress toward binding commercial agreements, conversion rates, or any indication that preliminary agreements translate to revenue contracts. Its continued use as a headline number without evolution is a pattern worth monitoring; if it remains static in Q2 and Q3 while the company continues to raise capital, it signals that the metric is being maintained for fundraising optics rather than as a genuine measure of commercial progress.

- **Financing uncertainty is disclosed but not quantified.** The combination of a 12-month minimum runway disclosure, a $40.3 million/quarter burn rate, and early-stage exploration of debt and strategic capital creates a financing risk that is technically disclosed but effectively minimized in the narrative. An investor who read only the prepared remarks would not grasp how central the capital question is to whether the Q1 2024 launch actually occurs.

- **The "historic achievement" framing is working against information density.** When every milestone is described as unprecedented or historic, investors lose the ability to calibrate which achievements are genuinely differentiating and which are expected table stakes. Avellan uses AT&T and Vodafone executive endorsements as credibility proxies in lieu of independent technical assessment—which may be appropriate at this stage, but creates dependence on opaque external validations.

- **ASIC timeline opacity is a latent risk.** Avellan says the ASICs are "at the very last end of development" and "nearing the phase where you go to [tape-out]." Block 2 satellites depend on them. The precision of "nearing" without a date, combined with the fact that this exact language has appeared in prior quarters, should prompt investors to ask directly whether this milestone is on schedule in the next call.

---

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **The CFO's depreciation disclosure is exemplary.** Proactively flagging that BlueWalker 3 is now "operational for accounting purposes" and that this will add "$17 million per quarter" in non-cash depreciation charges—before investors would have noticed—is precisely the kind of voluntary, consequential disclosure that the Rittenhouse framework credits. This is "What Went Wrong" reporting's cousin: "Here is an accounting change that will make our numbers look worse, and here is why."

- **Beam-pointing accuracy in Q&A is a model of technical candor.** When Mike Crawford asks about beamforming precision, Avellan answers with a specific, verifiable range ("within one, one and a half degrees to two and a half degrees depending on the size of the array") rather than a directional positive. This is the kind of specificity that distinguishes genuine technical leadership from promotional claims.

- **Founder alignment is disclosed concretely.** "Abel invested the initial risk capital to fund the business. He remains the largest shareholder in the company and does not draw a salary." This is specific, verifiable, and materially relevant to assessing whether management and shareholder interests are aligned. Most companies bury this in a proxy; placing it in the earnings call narrative signals it matters.

- **The SpaceX Falcon 9 launch agreement is appropriately specific.** "We signed an agreement in February of this year to launch our Block 1 satellites on a dedicated SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket in the first quarter of 2024." Named counterparty, specific vehicle, specific date, and signed agreement—not pipeline. This is the standard all capital commitment disclosures should be held to.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

**Claims to verify in Q2 2023 call:**
1. **ASIC completion:** Avellan described the ASICs as being at the "very last end of development" and "nearing tape-out." The Q2 call should either confirm tape-out was achieved or explain what delayed it. If this language repeats verbatim without update, it is a negative accountability signal.

2. **MNO agreement progression:** The "approximately 40 MNOs under preliminary agreements" figure should either increase, or management should be asked to explain what percentage have advanced beyond preliminary status toward binding commercial terms. If the number stagnates across quarters, the metric is decorative.

3. **Capital raising update:** Wallace described debt financing, export-import agency exploration, and strategic investor conversations as "preliminary." The Q2 call should show material progress on at least one of these channels, given the 12-month runway disclosure. If the same language recurs with no updates, investors should treat the financing risk as underpriced.

4. **Block 1 capex trajectory:** Wallace stated capex would "begin to increase" from the $10–13 million/quarter range to fund Block 1. The Q2 capex figure will be the first real-time validation of whether the $100–110 million total and Q1 2024 timeline remain achievable.

**First evaluation baseline:** This is the first analysis of this company in this framework. Future evaluations should assess whether the ratio of technical specificity (currently strong in Q&A) to promotional language (currently elevated in prepared remarks) shifts over time—and whether management begins to proactively revisit prior period commitments rather than only introducing new ones.
