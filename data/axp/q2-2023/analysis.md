# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: American Express | Quarter: Q2 2023 | Date: July 20, 2023

---

### Executive Summary

This is a high-quality earnings call communication that reflects a mature, well-run investor relations operation. Steve Squeri and Jeff Campbell speak with specificity, acknowledge trade-offs honestly, and link results to prior commitments with consistency — qualities that are rarer than they should be. The call is not without minor FOG, particularly in prepared remarks where some platitudes surface, but the substance-to-fluff ratio is strongly positive and the Q&A is notably responsive. A thoughtful long-term investor would leave this call with a clear understanding of the business model, its current headwinds, management's honest assessment of what is working and what is not, and confidence that the leadership team is running the company for the long term rather than managing the quarterly narrative.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: A**

Campbell's financial presentation is a model of specificity and contextual disclosure. He provides exact figures across all major lines — revenues of $15.1 billion (up 13% FX-adjusted), EPS of $2.89 (up 12%), pretax pre-provision income of $3.9 billion (up 33%), reserve build of $327 million, provision expense of $1.2 billion, and ending reserves of $4.7 billion representing 2.6% of total loans — and he links each number to prior guidance and expectations. The disclosure that reserves remain "about 30 basis points below the levels we had pre-pandemic or Day 1 CECL" is exactly the kind of benchmark contextualization that separates genuine transparency from number-dropping. Capital return is reported precisely: "$1.6 billion of capital returned in Q2, including $1.1 billion in buybacks and $446 million in dividends," with the CET1 ratio of 10.6% cited against both the stated target range of 10-11% and the regulatory floor of 7%. The addition of a new FICO disclosure — "only 8% of U.S. Card Member loans and receivables comes from customers with a FICO less than 660" — is a proactive risk disclosure made before it was forced, which is a strong positive signal. Non-GAAP usage is minimal and where it appears (FX-adjusted figures), the adjustments are explained. The only reason this falls short of a perfect score is that the full-year guidance range of $11.00–$11.40 EPS is confirmed without updating the midpoint or narrowing the range despite being six months into the year, which is a minor accountability gap.

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: A**

The strategy can be summarized in two sentences after reading this transcript, which is a demanding test: American Express runs a fee-based, spend-centric model targeting premium consumers (with Millennials and Gen Z as the primary acquisition cohort), small businesses, and network coverage expansion — funded by card fees (subscription-like), discount revenue, and net interest income. Results are consistently linked back to the January 2022 growth plan that promised 10%+ revenue growth and mid-teens EPS growth, and Squeri explicitly acknowledges where the strategy has performed differently than expected: "International has come back probably even a little quicker than we had thought... millennials are playing out even better than what we had thought... small business started out very strong for us last year, it's a little bit more muted." This is genuine retrospective accountability — not triumphalism. The admission that "did it play out exactly as we thought it was going to play out? No" followed by an honest performance-to-expectation comparison (original 2022 guidance was 17-19%, actual was 25%) is exactly the kind of "what went wrong and right" reporting Rittenhouse identifies as a trust signal. The 10-year Hilton extension, cited with counterparty name, scope, and duration, is a signed commitment rather than aspirational pipeline. The strategy discussion correctly identifies SME softness as an industry-wide phenomenon while owning Amex's exposure to it rather than deflecting — "would we prefer that our SME business was growing like our consumer business, sure, we would."

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: B**

The call addresses investors with precision and employees with some but not exceptional specificity. Squeri's acknowledgment of "77,000 colleagues across American Express" is slightly warmer than the standard boilerplate, and the extended Jeff Campbell tribute is personal, specific, and clearly genuine rather than HR-drafted. The customer lens is real: Squeri's explanation of the pandemic-era decision to invest in customer value propositions rather than drop costs to the bottom line — "it would have been easier to drop more money to the bottom line rather than put more money into our existing customers, but that's not how we run the company" — is a meaningful culture signal. Merchant/partner relationships are represented through the Hilton example with authentic long-term framing, and the GNS/Square discussion acknowledges the legitimate technical friction partners face in joining the network. The score does not reach A because creator/supply-side partner tensions are not acknowledged (the call describes Amex's network partners only positively), and employee contributions beyond the aggregate headcount figure are not named with specifics. Small business customer needs are described somewhat from the customer's perspective, but the language occasionally slips into "they are a vector for billings" framing. The voice is consistent and authentic — this does not read as ghostwritten.

---

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: B**

The call is substantially cleaner than a typical large-cap financial services transcript. The prepared remarks contain occasional standard FOG — "virtuous cycle of growth," "best-in-class credit performance," "continued strong demand," "great opportunities that are out there" — but these instances are outnumbered by specific, substantive claims in the same passages. Q&A is notably responsive: when analysts attempt to extract a more pessimistic read on billings or revenues, both Squeri and Campbell engage the actual question rather than pivoting to talking points. Campbell's response on the service fee revenue noise in Betsy Graseck's question is technically specific and directly addresses the analytical concern raised. The most significant FOG instance is Squeri's response to Rick Shane's acceleration question: "We still feel really confident that we'll be within the 15% to 17%. And there's really not a lot more to say other than that" — which is a mild deflection substituting assertion for explanation. However, it is immediately followed by enough contextual support to avoid being disqualifying. The call does not cross into dismissiveness or superlative abuse. Jeff Campbell's comment that he is "actually even more excited" about Amex's growth prospects than when he joined is the kind of warm unsupported assertion that earns a FOG flag but is proportionally minor given the surrounding specificity.

*Three FOG instances worth noting:*
1. "A business model that's differentiated from others in the industry, which gives us some important advantages" — asserted without enumeration at that point in the remarks
2. "We continue to be thoughtful about who we grow with and how" — vague claim without specifics
3. "We feel really good about it" — appears five or more times as a coda to substantive responses, functioning as a verbal tic that substitutes comfort for evidence

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: A**

This call demonstrates genuine long-term orientation that is backed by described behavior, not just stated intention. The pandemic investment story — maintaining and expanding card value propositions when a short-term financial manager would have cut them — is the strongest long-term signal in the transcript: "it was really important to embrace those customers. It would have been easier to drop more money to the bottom line rather than to put more money into our existing customers." This reveals a preference under pressure, which is the most credible kind of vision evidence. The multi-year growth aspiration framework (introduced January 2022) is revisited with honest progress accounting at six-quarter intervals, and Squeri's acknowledgment that the strategy "didn't play out exactly as we thought" — followed by explanation of what diverged and why results still met the underlying objective — demonstrates dualistic thinking rather than promotional optimism. The vision is specific enough to be falsifiable: "annual revenue growth in excess of 10% and mid-teens EPS growth in 2024 and beyond in a steady state macroenvironment" is a concrete commitment with a named economic condition attached. The CFO transition is handled with unusual transparency: Campbell's comment that "Christophe was in the room for every key decision we made as we developed that plan" signals intentional continuity rather than a disorienting reset. Investor education is implicit but real — the three-legged revenue model (card fees, discount revenue, net interest income) is explained repeatedly and clearly. The one limitation: the vision does not reach the level of specificity (e.g., target card member count, coverage penetration goals) that would move it from excellent to exemplary.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **Guidance confirmation without refinement.** At the midpoint of the fiscal year, Amex reconfirmed the full-year guidance range ($11.00–$11.40 EPS; 15–17% revenue growth) without narrowing it. For a company this far into its year with this level of visibility, the unchanged range is mildly evasive — particularly given that Q1 was "above trend" (Squeri's own framing) and H2 billings are expected to decelerate. A tighter range would be more accountable.

- **SME softness framed as industry-wide without sufficient evidence.** Squeri characterizes U.S. small business spending deceleration (2% growth) as "not an Amex phenomenon" and "a little bit of an industry phenomenon." This may well be true, but no peer data or external validation is offered for the claim. If Amex-specific underwriting tightening is also contributing (Campbell hints at this: "we have consistently made some adjustments from a risk management perspective"), the industry-wide framing partially obscures an internal driver.

- **"We feel really good about it" pattern.** The phrase "feel really good" or close variants appears with enough frequency to function as a verbal placeholder that closes off analytical engagement. This is a minor concern but worth tracking — if it intensifies in future quarters coinciding with worsening results, it would be a meaningful signal.

- **CFO transition disclosure is thin on Christophe's analytical approach.** Le Caillec's introduction consists of two sentences ("I'm excited to continue the strong legacy of performance that Steve and Jeff have delivered. I look forward to spending time with many of you on the call in the coming months."). For a transition of this significance, investors received essentially no signal about whether the incoming CFO's communication style, financial philosophy, or strategic perspectives differ from his predecessor's. This is a minor transparency gap with a potential for forward-period drift.

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **The pandemic investment disclosure is exceptional candor.** Squeri's unprompted explanation that Amex chose to sustain and expand customer value propositions during COVID — when cutting them would have been financially easier and analytically defensible — is rare CEO communication. It reveals governance philosophy, not just financial mechanics. Rittenhouse would give this high marks as evidence of genuine stewardship orientation.

- **Proactive risk disclosure (FICO band transparency).** The decision to add a new disclosure showing that only 8% of U.S. Card Member loans and receivables comes from FICO-below-660 customers — before any analyst asked about subprime exposure — is the kind of information leadership that builds long-term investor trust. The company answered a question the market was beginning to ask before it was asked.

- **Honest retrospective accountability on the growth plan.** Squeri's explicit comparison of where results landed versus original 2022 guidance expectations, including acknowledgment that the strategy "didn't play out exactly as we thought," is textbook candor. This is not easy to say publicly when results happened to be better than plan — it would have been simpler to take full credit.

- **Campbell's Basel III response is a model of structured reassurance.** Rather than simply reassuring investors that "we don't see Basel III as a material issue," Campbell provides three distinct reasons (regulatory minimum of 7% vs. 10-11% target, 30%+ ROE enabling rapid capital replenishment, and the multi-directional impact of different Basel components) that allow a sophisticated investor to verify the claim independently. This is investor education, not investor management.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

**Commitments to verify next quarter (Q3 2023):**
1. **Revenue guidance:** Full-year 2023 guidance of 15–17% revenue growth and EPS of $11.00–$11.40 should be either confirmed with narrowed range or revised with honest explanation. Watch for whether the range tightens as H2 progresses.
2. **T&E spending:** Campbell stated explicitly that T&E growth rate "remain[s] in the double-digits through the rest of this year." This is a specific, falsifiable forecast — verify against Q3 actuals.
3. **Credit normalization trajectory:** Write-off rates were described as expected to "continue to move up a bit" but remain "below pre-pandemic levels in 2023." Delinquency rates were "flat quarter-over-quarter." Track both against these forecasts.
4. **Marketing spend:** Full-year target of approximately $5.5 billion should be on track. With $1.4B in Q2, that implies roughly $1.35-1.45B per quarter in H2 — flag any material deviation.
5. **Christophe Le Caillec's communication style:** Q3 2023 will be Le Caillec's first full quarter as CFO. Evaluate whether the level of financial specificity and analytical depth established by Campbell is maintained, enhanced, or deteriorates. Changes in disclosure granularity across comparable periods would be a material signal.
6. **SME trajectory:** Squeri framed U.S. SME growth (2% in Q2) as cyclical and industry-wide. Verify whether this characterization holds against Q3 data and whether any peer commentary aligns with or contradicts the industry-wide explanation.

*This appears to be the first evaluation of American Express under this framework. A trend assessment will be possible beginning with the Q3 2023 analysis.*
