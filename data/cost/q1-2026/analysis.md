# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: Costco Wholesale Corporation | Quarter: Q1 FY2026 | Date: December 10, 2025

---

### Executive Summary

This was a competent, professionally executed earnings call with meaningfully above-average financial specificity and a genuine commitment to giving investors verifiable data points. The call is not exceptional—it rarely reaches the level of the most transparent companies in the Rittenhouse framework—but it consistently clears the bar of substantive disclosure and avoids the worst forms of FOG. The CFO (Millerchip) carried most of the analytical weight and demonstrated honest accounting of headwinds; the CEO (Vachris) contributed operational color and strategic conviction, though his prepared remarks occasionally traded specificity for enthusiasm, particularly on technology and AI.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: A-**

Millerchip provided a disciplined, granular income statement walkthrough—a rarity in modern earnings calls. He reported net income of $2.001 billion ($4.50/diluted share), itemized the discrete tax benefit ($72M, $0.06/share), and restated the comparable to "net income and EPS both grew 13.6%" excluding those items, which is precisely the kind of like-for-like reconciliation investors need. Comparable sales were specified to the decimal (6.4%, with and without gas/FX adjustment), and the 7.1% figure excluding gas was added unprompted for further clarity. The SG&A discussion was particularly candid: Millerchip named four distinct headwinds—wage agreement (mid-single-digit incremental), extended operating hours, healthcare costs ("the first quarter where we've seen healthcare costs grow at a faster pace than our sales"), and a four-basis-point tax assessment charge—and then explicitly stated the counterfactual: without those last two items, the company would have seen "mid single digit positive leverage." Full-year capex guidance of approximately $6.5 billion was provided. The one gap preventing a full A is the absence of explicit prior guidance revisitation—the company does not provide formal quarterly guidance, so this is structurally limited, but the result is that accountability to forward promises happens implicitly (through monthly sales releases) rather than explicitly on the call.

---

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: B+**

Costco's core strategic logic—buy in bulk, pass savings to members, grow membership, expand warehouse count—is sufficiently well-established that the call functions as a progress update against a stable, understood framework, which is itself a positive signal. Vachris provided specific evidence of strategic evolution: new warehouse productivity ($192M annualized in year of opening vs. $150M two years prior) is the most compelling single data point in the call and represents exactly the kind of falsifiable, benchmarked progress reporting that earns credit. The Baldwin Hills (Los Angeles) project and the Toulouse, France hypermart conversion are concrete examples of strategic adaptation, not just assertions of "creativity." However, the digital strategy section was materially weaker on specificity: "deliver a seamless experience that builds trust and loyalty" and "using technology to strengthen the fundamentals" are directionally correct but not measurable. No digital revenue targets, no defined milestones for personalization maturity, no framework for when digital transitions from investment to contributor. The strategy is clear; the milestones for new initiatives are largely qualitative.

---

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: B**

Costco's business model embeds member-centricity structurally, and the call reflects this authentically. Multiple specific examples of member value delivery were cited: named price reductions (KS chicken pot pie from $4.29 to $3.99/lb, KS bacon from $18.99 to $16.99), specific new Kirkland Signature items (caramelized blueberry croissants, dry facial towels), and holiday food records that convey operational pride rather than pure financial framing. The 15-20% value differential claim for KS vs. national brands is a specific, verifiable assertion that treats the investor as capable of evaluating evidence. Employee acknowledgment was present ("our operators did a great job improving productivity") but never moved beyond the generic—no named teams, no specific individuals, no examples of how particular employee decisions drove results. Supply-side partner relations (suppliers in the KS ecosystem, real estate developers, Instacart/Uber Eats/DoorDash) were mentioned functionally but not relationally. The voice is mostly authentic, particularly Vachris's operational examples, but the call remains predominantly investor/analyst-facing.

---

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: B**

For an earnings call of this length, the FOG density is well below average. Millerchip in particular demonstrated disciplined language—when he stated headwinds, he quantified them; when he described renewal rate dynamics, he acknowledged what he doesn't know while being specific about what he does ("we are still at a lower renewal rate on digital sign ups than we are on warehouse sign ups"). Q&A responses were largely responsive to the questions asked, with minimal pivot-and-deflect behavior. Vachris's prepared remarks were the weaker zone. Examples of FOG:

1. "Our digital vision at Costco is to deliver a seamless experience that builds trust and loyalty with our members both in warehouse and online." — Generic mission language that could describe any retailer.
2. "This isn't about technology for technology's sake. It's about using technology to strengthen the fundamentals that make Costco who we are." — The disclaimer about not doing "technology for technology's sake" is itself an Orwellian non-statement; it conveys no information.
3. "We're extremely excited about what the future holds for us" (on AI) — enthusiasm without content; immediately followed by slightly more specific examples, but the opener sets a poor tone.
4. "We see many opportunities that are really business driven and tangible and have great tangible business value for us." — The word "tangible" used twice in one sentence to mean nothing measurable.
5. "We think AI has a great asset to that and it really can help us become a much better merchant out there." — Syntactically awkward and substantively empty.

The FOG is concentrated in the technology/AI sections. The core business sections (financial results, real estate, inflation, memberships) were substantially cleaner.

---

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: B**

The long-term orientation is structurally embedded in Costco's model and consistently reflected in the call: "30 plus net openings per year in future years," a five-to-ten-year real estate planning horizon, and the explicit willingness to take lower short-term returns on new market entry in exchange for member base growth. Vachris demonstrated genuine forward-looking specificity on real estate—naming the Baldwin Hills project, the opening year (2027), and explaining precisely why the model works there ("a market we would never be able to go into and find 25 acres"). The $192M vs. $150M warehouse productivity comparison across cohorts is a multi-year trend, not a quarter-in-isolation claim. The 13.6% EPS growth figure (normalized) is respectable but not the focus of the leadership narrative—the focus is consistently on member value creation as the driver of long-term financial returns, which reflects coherent long-term stewardship thinking. What prevents an A: no acknowledgment of anything that went wrong or fell short. The Spain warehouse delays were mentioned matter-of-factly (reduced 2026 openings from 30 to 28) but without any ownership or explanation of what changed. The AI and digital sections offered vision without specificity—"we're extremely excited" and "great tangible business value" are not falsifiable forward claims. A Rittenhouse-standard A would include at least one voluntary disclosure of a miss or a miscalculation alongside the genuine long-term framing.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **FOG concentration in technology narrative:** Every time AI or digital strategy was discussed by Vachris, the language shifted from the specific-and-operational to the vague-and-aspirational. Given that technology investment is a stated strategic priority and a meaningful component of $6.5B capex, the absence of any measurable technology milestone creates a trust gap between stated importance and communicable evidence. This is a pattern to watch: if future calls continue to describe AI as "exciting" and "tangible" without any defined metrics, it signals that management may not yet have an internal accountability framework for these investments.

- **Membership renewal rate forecasting tone:** Millerchip explicitly said renewal rates "may still see a slight decline in the overall renewal rate over the next few quarters" while simultaneously describing encouraging early results from targeted communications. This creates a mild credibility hedge—the good news is credited to management action, while downside risk is attributed to structural math. Monitoring whether renewal rates actually stabilize over the next two to three quarters will reveal whether this framing was honest uncertainty management or conservative sandbagging.

- **No acknowledgment of any business shortfall:** The call contained zero voluntary disclosure of anything that went wrong, underperformed, or was misjudged. Spain delays were mentioned factually but not owned. Healthcare cost acceleration was disclosed but presented as an industry phenomenon. In a Rittenhouse framework, a consistent absence of "what went wrong" reporting, even in a strong quarter, is a mild negative signal about whether leadership has the self-awareness to disclose problems voluntarily when they are material. One quarter is not a pattern; this warrants monitoring.

- **"Mid to high single digit" and other range language:** Throughout the call, directional ranges were used frequently in Q&A ("mid single digit," "low to mid single digit," "high single digit") without anchoring to specific numbers. While some imprecision in Q&A is expected, the accumulation of these phrases—rather than rounding to specific figures—suggests a slight preference for plausible deniability over crisp accountability.

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **Proactive four-item SG&A decomposition:** Millerchip's unprompted breakdown of the four specific headwinds to SG&A leverage—with counterfactual quantification—is textbook Rittenhouse-standard disclosure. He didn't wait for an analyst to ask why leverage was negative; he explained it clearly and showed what underlying performance looked like without one-time items.

- **New warehouse productivity cohort comparison ($192M vs. $150M):** This single data point is one of the strongest pieces of disclosure on the call. It is specific, benchmarked across time, falsifiable, and directly relevant to evaluating the return on capital of the growth strategy. Credit Vachris for including it in prepared remarks rather than waiting for an analyst question.

- **Honest renewal rate framing with clear accountability:** Rather than papering over the renewal rate decline, Millerchip explained the mechanism (digitally-signed members skew younger, renew at lower rates), acknowledged the work remaining, and specifically flagged that future quarters could still show declines. This is the kind of transparent uncertainty management that builds durable investor trust.

- **Specific price reduction examples with precise figures:** Calling out KS chicken pot pie ($4.29 to $3.99/lb), KS bacon ($18.99 to $16.99), and KS walnuts ($14.49 to $12.99) treats investors as intelligent adults interested in operational evidence, not just comps percentages. These are verifiable. A member can check them. This is the opposite of FOG.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

**Commitments and claims to verify next quarter:**
1. **Renewal rate stabilization:** Millerchip said the Q1 decline was "less than anticipated" due to targeted communications, while cautioning that further slight declines are possible over "the next few quarters." Track the renewal rate in Q2 and Q3 FY2026. If it continues declining despite claimed improvements, the optimistic framing this quarter will need to be reweighted downward.
2. **Full-year warehouse openings (28 net new + 5 relocations):** Verify the cadence of openings. With Spain delays already taken, any further compression would represent a second revision and warrant scrutiny of the real estate team's execution capacity.
3. **Digital sales growth pace vs. overall comps:** Multiple claims were made that digital "would continue to grow at a fast pace over the longer term than average sales overall." Site traffic (+24%) and app traffic (+48%) are directional; actual digital sales growth rate (20.5% comp in Q1) should be tracked for deceleration or acceleration.
4. **Healthcare cost trend:** Millerchip identified healthcare costs growing faster than sales as a "first quarter" phenomenon. If this persists into Q2 without named mitigation actions, the Q1 framing of it as manageable will be questioned.
5. **AI/technology milestones:** No specific milestones were stated, which makes accountability difficult—but future calls should be checked for whether "pay ahead for pharmacy," "ordering cakes and deli trays online," and the app roadmap items Vachris mentioned translate into specific traffic or engagement metrics.

**Trend note (first evaluation):** This is the initial evaluation of Costco under this framework. The benchmark is set: strong financial disclosure, moderate strategic specificity on new initiatives, below-average "what went wrong" candor, and a technology narrative that is presently aspirational. Future calls should be evaluated for whether the digital/AI language becomes more specific or remains stuck at the "exciting" level.
