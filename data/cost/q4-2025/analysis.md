# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: Costco Wholesale Corporation | Quarter: Q4 Fiscal Year 2025 | Date: September 24, 2025

---

### Executive Summary

This was a competent, professionally delivered earnings call that meets the basic obligations of investor disclosure without meaningfully exceeding them. Gary Millerchip provided solid financial detail in the prepared remarks, while Ron Vachris brought authentic operational texture to strategic questions. The communication is honest and consistent with Costco's track record, but it leans on hedged forward language, stops short of explicit accountability to prior commitments, and rarely forces the audience to think harder about the business. A thoughtful long-term investor would come away informed but not educated.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: B**

Millerchip opened with crisp financial specifics: net income of $2.61 billion or $5.87 per diluted share (up 14% ex-tax item), net sales of $84.43 billion, comp sales of 5.7% (6.4% adjusted), and e-commerce comps of 13.6%. The membership fee income decomposition was a genuine strength — breaking out the fee-increase contribution ("a little less than half"), FX adjustment (13.6%), and underlying base growth (7%) across three distinct drivers. Capital expenditure was disclosed at "a little under $5.5 billion" for the full year with a qualitative explanation of what drove the increase (warehouse growth, remodels, depot expansion, manufacturing), but no specific 2026 CapEx figure was provided — Millerchip deferred that to Q1, which is consistent with historical practice and therefore not a negative signal. The LIFO charge ($43 million vs. an $8 million credit a year ago) was explained with context and acknowledged as consistent with prior guidance, which is good accountability. What's missing for an A: no explicit revisitation of prior-year guidance targets, no segment-level operating margin disclosure, and the forward CapEx framing ("probably a little bit higher than sales again") is directional rather than quantitative.

---

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: B**

Costco's strategy is coherent and repeatable across calls: deliver maximum member value through limited SKU, Kirkland Signature penetration, operational efficiency, and warehouse expansion. Ron Vachris's answer on holiday assortment was strategically specific and candid about a real pivot — thinning seasonal/trim categories to create space for higher-ticket items like backyard sheds and saunas, with honest acknowledgment that "it's not gonna be a marked change than what you'll see in a Costco." The tariff response was unusually well-structured, with multiple named strategies (global consolidation, country-of-origin switching, KS substitution, domestic sourcing) and two concrete data points: "30 to 40% on the cost of items by consolidating" and "40% in Asia by moving production." Ron's framing — "the last effect would be we pass on price... always the first one to go down" — is a clean, verifiable strategic commitment that can be tested next quarter. The grade stops at B because the digital strategy discussion remains abstract ("multiyear journey," "foundation elements of our plan") without milestones or measurable targets, and the B2B/business center opportunity, while acknowledged, received no quantification or timeline.

---

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: B**

Employee recognition appears in SG&A commentary ("our operators continue to do a great job leveraging strong top-line sales and improving labor productivity") and in the specific call-out that warehouse hour changes were implemented without material SG&A impact — a concrete result that implies operational workforce competence. The March employee agreement and July off-cycle wage increase are disclosed with specific basis-point impacts (mid-single-digit and mid-to-high single-digit, respectively), which demonstrates transparency about labor costs that many retailers obscure. The member-centric framing is genuine and consistent — "how do we start every day thinking of how can we deliver more value for our members" — and Millerchip's explanation of supplier relationships (working "with suppliers to find offsets and efficiencies") is real rather than promotional. The "fun sales facts" segment (245 million hot dog combos, 157 million rotisserie chickens) is charming and culture-revealing in a way that feels authentic to Costco's identity. What prevents an A: supplier/creator partner relationships are discussed purely as cost-mitigation levers during tariff navigation, with no acknowledgment of the partner's perspective, and investor-centric communication still dominates the Q&A.

---

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: B**

The overall substance-to-fluff ratio is positive. Millerchip in particular uses precise language in the prepared remarks and most Q&A answers are directly responsive to the questions asked. The tariff response is a model of layered, specific disclosure. However, FOG appears with moderate regularity, particularly in forward-looking and strategic passages, and the Q&A occasionally drifts toward talking-point pivots rather than direct answers.

**FOG examples (for grading transparency):**

1. *"We continue to see significant opportunities for expansion both domestically and internationally across the markets where we currently operate."* — This sentence contains no information a listener did not already possess.

2. *"Our focus tends to be a little bit less on the way you phrase the question and more on how do we make sure that's always at the center of everything we do."* (Millerchip, responding to Kelly Bania on US household penetration) — A classic deflection. The question asked for an estimate of current US household penetration and a ceiling. The answer provided neither.

3. *"It's a multiyear journey, and as we complete the foundation elements of our plan, we are able to launch new experiences for members."* — Temporal vagueness dressed as strategic communication.

4. *"We have a tremendously successful travel business that delivers significant value for members."* — Unsupported superlative with no accompanying metric.

5. *"We still think there's opportunity to continue to grow that base."* — Millerchip's response to membership growth sustainability is directionally correct but substantively empty; after three sentences of framing, no new information was delivered.

The Q&A on membership renewal rate (Michael Lasser question) was the most structurally evasive exchange on the call — Millerchip provided good analysis of *why* the rate is declining but explicitly declined to answer whether it could return to the mid-to-high 80s range, redirecting to overall membership fee income instead.

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: B**

Costco's long-term orientation is expressed through action more than language, which is generally the more trustworthy form. The decision to grow CapEx faster than sales for the first time "in a while" — with explicit investment in remodels for a warehouse base averaging twenty years of age — reflects genuine long-term thinking rather than quarter-to-quarter optimization. The manufacturing investments (hot dog production expansion, new coffee roasting facility) are specific, somewhat unusual disclosures that reveal an owner-operator mentality. Ron Vachris's comment that "we don't strive for a number" on warehouse openings — explicitly rejecting growth-for-growth's-sake — is a meaningful signal of capital discipline. The "first to go down" commitment on pricing when tariff pressure eases is a falsifiable vision statement. Where the call falls short of an A: there is no explicit revisitation of prior-year targets or goals (what did management say it would accomplish in fiscal 2025, and did it?), no acknowledgment of anything that went wrong or underperformed, and the digital vision ("multiyear journey," "opportunities to improve") lacks the specificity that would make it falsifiable or trackable.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **Renewal rate deflection pattern.** Millerchip twice declined to answer the specific membership renewal rate question (Lasser and Grom both circled it), pivoting each time to aggregate membership fee income. The framing "a few more quarters of a similar type of impact" establishes a vague runway without a floor. If the rate continues to decline beyond what is attributable to online mix, the current framing will have provided no accountability anchor.

- **CapEx deferral without a ceiling.** Millerchip confirmed CapEx will grow faster than sales again in fiscal 2026 but deferred the specific number to Q1 "as we do every year." While procedurally consistent, investors received no range or framework for modeling the spend — particularly notable given that Q4 CapEx of $1.97 billion ran at an elevated pace that was partially explained by one-time acceleration for 2026 openings.

- **Digital strategy lacks falsifiable milestones.** The retail media and digital personalization roadmap is described consistently as a "journey" and "early stages" across multiple calls without measurable interim targets. The Kimberly-Clark campaign result (14:1 ROAS, 45% digital sales lift) is a strong specific data point, but it is presented as an illustration rather than a baseline. There is no stated target for media revenue, supplier count, or capability completion date.

- **Holiday assortment pivot carries execution risk that was framed exclusively as opportunity.** Vachris's answer on the holiday mix change was candid and strategically coherent, but the communication presented only the upside (new categories performing well) with no acknowledgment of the risk that thinning core seasonal merchandise could disappoint loyal members expecting the traditional Costco holiday treasure hunt.

---

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **Tariff response was the call's communication highlight.** The multipronged framework (absorb costs, supplier negotiation, global consolidation, country-of-origin switching, assortment rotation, KS substitution) with two specific cost-reduction data points (30–40% global consolidation savings, 40% Asia production savings) is exactly the kind of substantive, evidence-based disclosure that the Rittenhouse framework rewards. Ron Vachris's "last to go up, first to go down" framing is a voluntarily binding commitment.

- **Membership fee income decomposition.** Breaking one metric into three components (fee increase, FX, underlying base growth) with separate percentages for each, and then further isolating the online sign-up mix effect on renewal rates, is investor-education at its best. A general investor is meaningfully smarter about Costco's membership economics after this passage.

- **LIFO acknowledgment and consistency.** Disclosing a $43 million LIFO charge, contextualizing it against the prior year's $8 million credit, and explicitly noting it was "essentially in line with the estimate we provided last quarter" is a small but meaningful act of accountability. It tells investors the company tracks its own forward estimates and reports back against them.

- **Authentic operational specificity from Vachris.** The holiday assortment pivot (backyard sheds, saunas, furniture replacing thinned seasonal), the business center expansion logic (60% delivery, appropriate parking for B2B), and the cannibalization management commentary all carry the texture of someone who actually runs the business, not someone reading from a briefing document.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

1. **Membership renewal rate floor.** Millerchip committed to "a few more quarters of a similar type of impact" (approximately 40 basis points of decline). Track whether the Q1 and Q2 fiscal 2026 renewal rates decline by approximately 40 basis points each, and whether management revisits this commitment when reporting those results. Any renewal rate decline exceeding 50 basis points, or any language shift away from "a few more quarters," should be flagged.

2. **CapEx fiscal 2026 number.** Management deferred the specific figure to Q1. Verify in Q1 that the disclosed number is (a) provided as promised and (b) consistent with the directional guidance given tonight ("grow over 2025, probably a little bit higher than sales again").

3. **Tariff pricing commitment.** Ron Vachris stated Costco will be "the last one to go up and always the first one to go down" on prices affected by tariffs. Track any reported price increases on tariff-affected SKUs and compare timing against peers. This is a specific, falsifiable commitment.

4. **Digital/media revenue milestones.** The Kimberly-Clark campaign result (14:1 ROAS) was positioned as proof-of-concept for a broader media business. Track whether Q1 or Q2 calls include any quantitative target for supplier media partnerships, media revenue contribution, or the completion timeline for the "unified single data platform."

5. **Holiday assortment performance.** Vachris described the pivot (sheds, saunas, furniture replacing seasonal trim) as "very exciting" with early positive signals. Q1 fiscal 2026 should provide the first full read on whether the non-traditional holiday assortment drove the expected top-line offset. Compare nonfood comps in Q1 against Q4's high-single-digit result.
