# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: Costco Wholesale Corporation | Quarter: Q1 Fiscal 2025 (12 weeks ended November 24, 2024) | Date: December 11, 2024

---

### Executive Summary

This is a high-quality earnings call from a company whose communication culture matches its business culture: disciplined, specific, and member-centric without being evasive about complexity or uncertainty. Gary Millerchip (CFO, new to the role from Kroger) delivers meticulous financial detail with appropriate context, while Ron Vachris (CEO) adds operational texture and authentic voice. The call's primary weakness is a modest reliance on Costco's characteristic operational storytelling ("fun facts") that occasionally substitutes anecdote for strategic precision—though this is a minor critique against an otherwise candid baseline.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: A**

Millerchip opens with a comprehensive, layered financial summary that exemplifies what good disclosure looks like. Net income is reported both as-reported and adjusted for the discrete $100M tax item ("excluding these discrete tax items, net income and earnings per diluted share grew 9.9% and 9.8%, respectively"), proactively removing a flattering one-time benefit from the headline number—a move that builds rather than exploits trust. Comp sales are provided five ways (US, Canada, international, total, e-commerce) and further disaggregated by the FX and gas deflation headwinds ("gas price deflation negatively impacted sales by approximately 1.6%"), so investors can see both the reported and underlying business. Capital allocation is addressed directly: "$5 billion" full-year CapEx estimate, the special dividend's known impact on interest income headwinds ("interest income faced headwinds in the quarter due to lower cash balances, subsequent to our special dividend in January 2024"), and granular segment-level gross margin commentary that breaks core-on-core margins into Foods (flat), Nonfoods (down slightly), and Fresh (up slightly). The retail media disclosure is appropriately cautious ("very much in the early innings"), avoiding the temptation to inflate a nascent opportunity. The one modest gap: forward guidance is structural rather than quantitative—CapEx range is given but no revenue or EPS targets are offered, consistent with Costco's historical practice but still a ceiling on the A.

---

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: B**

Costco's strategy is genuinely simple and coherently executed: grow the warehouse base (26 net new per year, ~30% international), invest the membership fee back into lower prices and better products, and expand e-commerce and ancillary businesses as member value amplifiers. This comes through clearly and consistently. Millerchip's framing of the membership fee reinvestment is notably holistic and explicit: "It's partly where is it that we can lower prices...Part of it's where can we mitigate the impact of inflation...It's how can we improve the membership experience including investing in our employees' wages...And, of course, innovation with new products through Kirkland Signature." Specific price reductions are cited by SKU with dollar amounts (Kirkland Signature organic peanut butter: $11.49 → $9.99), which is accountability in practice—management is showing, not just saying, that they invest in price. The warehouse pipeline (29 openings, 3 relocations, 10 international) is quantified. The primary reason this doesn't reach A is that milestones are rarely explicitly revisited against prior targets. For instance, Kirkland Signature private label penetration (now 33% in the US) is reported when asked, but there's no stated prior goal it was measured against. The e-commerce strategy is described more as a "journey" than a destination with falsifiable targets ("we still believe there's more work to do" is honest but not measurable).

---

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: A**

This call distinguishes itself in stakeholder balance. Employees are acknowledged by headcount ("over 330,000 employees around the world") and by category (cashiers, pharmacy staff, buyers, operators, logistics teams) with specifics, not platitudes. The Teamsters response is a case study in honest stakeholder communication: Vachris does not hide behind legal language or deflect with generalities—he says directly, "We have a forty-year track record of dealing fairly with the Teamsters union, and really nothing has changed about that. These are Costco employees. They're of great importance to us." This is an authentic, ownable statement on a sensitive topic with real business risk. Members are the axis of nearly every answer—the framing consistently inverts to the member's perspective ("what are we paying for the membership fee each year that we're delivering more value"). Suppliers and vendors are acknowledged through the price-investment narrative (asking vendors to "contribute with lower prices" while Costco demonstrates it first through Kirkland). Investors receive dense, usable financial disclosure. The call mentions the sustainability report in opening remarks, acknowledging broader stakeholders without making it performative. The one note: creators/partners (e.g., CPG brands participating in retail media) are described primarily as revenue opportunities rather than as partners with interests of their own—minor for a retailer, but worth noting.

---

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: B**

The predominant register of this call is precise and functional, which puts it above most retail earnings calls. Q&A answers are notably responsive—when Christopher Horvers asks what's in "hardware," Vachris simply lists: "storage, plumbing, lighting, power tools, batteries." When Michael Lasser asks whether Costco is requiring higher margins from traditional vendors to offset low-margin traffic-driving items, the answer is a clean "No" with explanation, not a deflection. Millerchip's tariff response quotes his predecessor ("when it rains, it rains on everybody") and then provides a structured, honest framework for how Costco actually manages tariff risk (pull forward inventory, work with vendors, alternative sourcing, SKU substitution)—this is clear thinking communicated clearly. The FOG that does exist is concentrated in a few areas: the retail media commentary ("we're very much in the early innings" appears twice, and "incremental" is used three times as a one-word justification without quantification), and Vachris's prepared "fun facts" section prioritizes anecdote over strategic context. The Pleasanton opening day record ($2.9M) is vivid but disconnected from any broader metric about what "good" looks like for a new US warehouse. There is no meaningful Q&A evasion, which is the stronger signal.

**FOG examples (for documentation):**
1. "We're very much in the early innings with retail media" — repeated twice, offered as a substitute for any quantitative framing of the opportunity size or timeline.
2. "It's a journey. We're gonna start seeing some more forward-facing improvements in the next twelve months" — vague timeline with no specifics on what those improvements are.
3. "We're always keeping nimble and continue to change those categories" — grammatically awkward and semantically empty.
4. "Strong partnerships with both organizations [Instacart and Uber]" — the adjective "strong" carries no weight; what follows (complementary shopping behavior, cost efficiency) is substantive, but the opener is a cliché.

---

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: B**

Costco's long-term orientation is embedded in how they discuss nearly every decision—the e-commerce "back of the house" investments being made before front-end improvements ("speed, stability, making sure all the foundational work is done so we can build on that"), the patient approach to retail media ("just really embarking on that off-site journey"), the philosophy of returning membership fee value to members rather than expanding margin. Vachris's response on new warehouse incrementality is genuinely strategic: the Pleasanton example illustrates that relief warehouses drive net new traffic, not just cannibalization, and this logic is offered as the basis for continued US expansion confidence—not just assertion. The discussion of digital member renewal rates is exemplary long-term disclosure: rather than presenting 90.4% and moving on, Millerchip proactively explains the structural mix shift driving the decline and flags that it "will have a continued effect on our published renewal rate for the remainder of 2025." That is a CEO/CFO pair choosing transparency over optics. The grade doesn't reach A because there is no stated falsifiable vision—no stated goal for e-commerce penetration, no target member count, no margin framework. Costco manages this deliberately (they don't provide quarterly guidance), but the trade-off is that long-term accountability is harder to track. The "What Went Wrong" dimension is largely absent: inflation is described as "essentially flat," supply chain issues are described as managed, and no operational failures are surfaced. This is not dishonest—things appear to be going well—but a truly candid communication would find something that didn't work and address it directly.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **No explicit accountability loop on prior-quarter commitments.** Millerchip's November 2024 sales release referenced a 15% calendar headwind, and this was explained in Q&A only when an analyst asked about e-commerce growth. Investors reading only prepared remarks would have no context for the 13% e-commerce comp. A communication structure that proactively surfaces prior-period context items—rather than leaving them for Q&A—would reduce the information gap between active and passive investors.

- **Retail media is being described with enthusiasm but no framework for evaluation.** The "two to three times return on ad spend" figure for the first campaign is the only quantitative disclosure in what is described as "a significant growth opportunity in the future." Without a baseline or scale marker (one campaign with one CPG partner; 25 suppliers engaged), investors cannot assess whether management's enthusiasm is calibrated. The risk is that "early innings" becomes a permanent excuse to avoid accountability on a material revenue stream.

- **The digital renewal rate headwind is honest but incompletely bounded.** Millerchip explains the mix shift toward digital members who renew at lower rates, but he does not provide the actual renewal rate differential between digital and base members, nor does he quantify how large the digital cohort now is. Investors are told the direction of the effect, but cannot size it. This is a gap that could be filled without competitive harm.

- **Vachris's opening "fun facts" framing.** Presenting operational milestones (pizza records, pie records, prescription growth) as "fun facts" is charming and humanizing—but it also means these metrics exist outside any evaluative framework. Is 19% prescription growth a function of competitor disruption, Costco-specific investments, or general category growth? "Fun facts" signals the right instinct (share the business broadly) but slight presentation discipline would make these figures more analytically useful.

---

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **Proactive tax item disaggregation.** Opening the call by stripping out the $100M discrete tax benefit before reporting headline growth ("excluding these discrete tax items, net income and earnings per diluted share grew 9.9% and 9.8%") is a genuine act of investor service. Many CFOs would lead with the headline EPS beat and footnote the tax item. Millerchip does the reverse.

- **Direct, responsive Q&A under genuine pressure.** The Teamsters labor question, tariff question, and "are you requiring more margin from other vendors" question each carried real business sensitivity. All three received direct, substantive answers without deflection. The tariff answer in particular—acknowledging costs rise, quoting a predecessor, and then walking through the actual mitigation toolkit—is a model of how to discuss uncertainty without hiding behind it.

- **SKU-level price investment disclosure.** Listing actual product names and price reductions (Kirkland Signature organic peanut butter $11.49 → $9.99; chicken stock $9.99 → $8.99; Sauvignon Blanc $7.49 → $6.99) is accountability in granular form. These are checkable. Management is inviting verification, not avoiding it.

- **Honest digital renewal rate disclosure.** Flagging a structural, self-inflicted (in a positive sense) headwind to a key reported metric—before analysts ask—and then explaining the mechanism and duration ("a continued effect on our published renewal rate for the remainder of 2025") is the kind of proactive candor that builds durable investor trust. Analysts who follow this company closely will note that Millerchip is establishing a clear causal story that can be tracked and verified.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

**Commitments to verify next quarter (Q2 FY2025, expected March 2025):**

1. **Retail media traction:** Management stated they are "working with over 25 suppliers" and described the off-site campaign as their "first." By Q2, investors should expect either a specific number of completed campaigns, a pipeline update with quantification, or an acknowledgment if traction has slowed. If the "early innings" language persists without any new metrics, that is a negative accountability signal.

2. **Membership renewal rate trajectory:** Millerchip explicitly predicted the digital mix shift will "continue to have a slight impact on that overall membership renewal rate for the remainder of 2025." Q2 will be the first test. If the rate drops further than "one-tenth of a percent," the explanation should evolve. If it stabilizes, that should be explained too.

3. **E-commerce penetration and front-end improvements:** Vachris committed to "front-facing improvements in the next twelve months" to the digital experience. Investors should look for whether Q2 or Q3 calls introduce specific new features or functionality, or whether this remains aspirational.

4. **Interest income headwinds:** Millerchip stated explicitly that the interest income headwind "will continue to negatively impact year-over-year comparisons in Q2." This is a trackable forward statement—verify that Q2 interest income is disclosed and contextualized against this prediction.

5. **Teamsters negotiation:** Vachris described an ongoing process without timeline. Any update—resolution, escalation, or continued status quo—should be disclosed proactively in Q2 rather than waiting for an analyst question.

**First evaluation—no prior trend data available.** Costco's communications culture appears historically stable and above-average. Future evaluations should monitor whether Millerchip's financial disclosure style (which appears more granular than typical for a retailer CFO) is sustained as he settles into the role, and whether the Vachris/Millerchip combination finds a more integrated voice across prepared remarks.
