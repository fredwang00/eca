# Earnings Call Candor Analysis
## Company: Affirm Holdings | Quarter: Q3 Fiscal 2025 | Date: May 7, 2025

---

### Executive Summary

Affirm's Q3 FY2025 call is a genuinely strong communication—specific, responsive, and largely free of the defensive posturing that characterizes many fintech calls. Max Levchin in particular demonstrates an unusual willingness to engage with complexity and risk in real time, including a mid-Q&A interjection to caution that "in the current moment, the snapshot looks fantastic. Things could change. We are very attentive." That kind of unsolicited candor is rare and meaningful. The primary communication weaknesses are modest: some quantitative specificity is withheld where it could reasonably be provided (zero APR RLTC contribution, Walmart trajectory, Costco impact), and the CFO's prepared remarks were notably brief—the financial picture is clearer in Q&A than it might have been with fuller prepared commentary.

---

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor
**Grade: B**

Rob O'Hare provides substantive financial texture throughout Q&A—citing "$100 million of GMV a day," noting the slight uptick in prepayments as a positive credit signal, explaining warrant amortization's impact on the sales and marketing line, and connecting the non-consolidated ABS deal to off-balance sheet funding mix. The guidance framework is specific: "at the high end of the GMV range, we're calling for 34% year-on-year growth, and that's down ever so slightly from the 36% growth that we saw this quarter." However, the prepared remarks from the CFO were strikingly brief—essentially a single paragraph—with no structured financial summary in prepared form. Critically, when asked what RLTC would have been absent the elevated zero APR mix, O'Hare declined: "We haven't given precision around that, Adam, other than to say, zeros are profitable for us." This is a reasonable position but still a gap. Capital allocation rationale is articulated (funding channels, balance sheet discipline, no bank charter for funding purposes) but not benchmarked against prior-period targets in any formal way. The "signed versus aspirational" test is partially passed: the Shopify renewal through 2028 is named and term-specific, Costco is named but explicitly undimensionized, and Walmart is acknowledged as live but its forward trajectory is deliberately left opaque.

---

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability
**Grade: A-/B+**

The strategic logic of Affirm's call is highly legible: grow GMV through broad merchant adoption, use zero APR as a brand-building and user-acquisition tool (particularly for the Affirm Card), protect credit quality as the non-negotiable constraint, and expand internationally via Shopify's platform. These pillars are restated consistently and connected to specific actions. Levchin's explanation of why 0% APR wins over 10% discounts in A/B tests is a model of evidence-backed strategic reasoning: "nine out of 10 times the 0% is just much more compelling even though it costs the merchants less than 10%." Michael Linford's competition answer is exemplary—he points investors to the earnings supplement's merchant fee rate trend chart as empirical evidence of pricing stability, a notably accountable gesture. The one accountability gap: prior-quarter commitments (e.g., app redesign metrics, international beta milestones) are revisited only qualitatively, with Levchin explicitly deferring ("ask me this question maybe next quarter"). That's honest, but it also means the call generates more forward promises than it closes prior ones. The Walmart situation represents a genuine strategic accountability gap that management navigates with appropriate caution but without full transparency.

---

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals
**Grade: B**

Levchin's extended discussion of credit bureau reporting is one of the most authentic stakeholder-centered passages in recent earnings call memory. He addresses consumers directly: "if I'm borrowing money and I'm paying it back on time, I want that reflected on my permanent record." He then explains the *work* done on their behalf—years of testing with TransUnion and Experian to ensure reporting "will not have weird consequences." This goes well beyond platitude. The invitation to competitors to adopt the same practice ("I invite all of our competitors to do the same thing") reads as genuine mission expression rather than competitive posturing. Employee contributions are not specifically recognized on this call, which is a modest gap. The merchant-as-partner framing is consistent and substantiated: Linford's description of merchants who run 0% promotions as "growth-oriented" and "front-footed" treats merchants as agents with genuine strategic interests rather than as revenue extraction targets. The investor audience is addressed competently but without the kind of investor education (e.g., explaining the capital markets flywheel or unit economics for a new analyst) that would push this to an A.

---

### 4. FOG Index — Linguistic Quality of Disclosure
**Grade: B+**

This is one of the lower-FOG calls in the BNPL/fintech space. Levchin in particular has a distinctive voice—direct, occasionally self-deprecating, willing to say "I don't know" or "that's not the right place to pre-announce." Q&A answers are overwhelmingly responsive to the actual question asked. The CFO's brief prepared remarks contain the most concentrated FOG in the call: "The quarter's results of course speak for themselves. We are pleased to improve our outlook...this quarter is a good example of us leaning into growth opportunities with excellent credit quality and LTV characteristics while hitting our targets." These sentences contain "leaning into," "excellent credit quality," and "hitting our targets"—three classic weasel phrases in two sentences. However, this is isolated and quickly eclipsed by substantive Q&A. Levchin's GenAI explanation ("I'll try to be very precise") and his unprompted recession-scenario framing ("things could change. We are very attentive. We will change with things") are anti-FOG moments. The phrase "brand halo" appears, which edges toward jargon, but is immediately followed by specific economic reasoning. No Orwellian nonsense detected.

**Residual FOG examples (for completeness):**
1. "The quarter's results of course speak for themselves" — a cliché that replaces explanation with assertion
2. "leaning into growth opportunities with excellent credit quality" — three vague qualifiers stacked
3. "we'll continue to be judicious in how we spend our hard-earned dollars" — corporate boilerplate
4. "brand deposit that we're making" — metaphor that sounds meaningful but is unquantified
5. "changing the world" (Linford, competition answer) — aspirational closer without substantiation

---

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation
**Grade: A-**

Levchin's long-term orientation is the strongest dimension of this call. His explanation of the zero APR strategy as a "lifetime value booster" with "delayed gratification" that "is totally worth it" reflects genuine multi-year thinking rather than quarter optimization. His bank charter answer is textbook strategic discipline: he first eliminates a popular misconception (a charter doesn't solve funding), then articulates a precise and falsifiable condition for reconsidering ("if there were a feature that we just couldn't build without a bank charter, we would absolutely consider pursuing one"). This is not a vision statement—it is a decision framework, which is actually more valuable. The app discussion treats repayment rate as a sacrosanct guardrail, which is a remarkable statement of values in a sector known for burying repayment UX to drive late fees. The GenAI framing ("you should absolutely expect us to have an answer for that as well") is appropriately confident without being specific enough to be binding. The one gap: no "what went wrong" disclosure. The call is almost entirely forward-looking and constructive; mistakes, near-misses, or lessons from prior quarters are not surfaced. This is a meaningful omission by Rittenhouse standards, though it does not dominate the communication.

### Communication-Level Risks

- **Prepared remarks are structurally thin.** The CFO's opening amounted to five sentences of boilerplate before handing directly to Q&A. This is a consistent pattern at Affirm—the company relies heavily on its shareholder letter to do the disclosure work, which is legitimate, but it means investors who only attend the call without reading the letter receive a materially incomplete picture. If letter quality ever declines, the call structure provides no redundancy.

- **Walmart opacity is a compounding risk.** Management's response to the Walmart question ("the day we understand when that changes and how, we'll obviously notify you and the market") is professionally appropriate but does not resolve the uncertainty. If Walmart is a material revenue contributor and its trajectory is genuinely unknown to management, that is a business risk. If it is known and being managed carefully through disclosure timing, that creates trust risk. The pattern of "we're being a good partner" without specifics is one to monitor.

- **Forward-commitment density is high.** This call generated a significant number of testable future claims: Shopify UK beta in "reasonable time," Costco going live in "reasonable future," app improvements visible "next quarter," AI capabilities that "you should absolutely expect us to have an answer for." When multiple commitments accumulate without prior-period accountability, the risk of the "perishable goal" pattern increases.

- **Zero APR mix opacity.** The repeated refusal to quantify the RLTC impact of elevated zero APR mix ("we haven't given precision around that") is understandable given genuine complexity, but it is the single most-asked question on the call. When the most analytically relevant number is consistently withheld, it creates an information gap that the market tends to fill pessimistically.

---

### Notable Communication Strengths

- **Unsolicited risk acknowledgment.** Levchin's mid-Q&A interjection—"I just wanted to comment that it's really important to be intellectually honest about credit. In the current moment, the snapshot looks fantastic. Things could change."—is precisely the kind of voluntary disclosure that Rittenhouse identifies as the strongest trust signal. It costs management nothing economically but everything reputationally if wrong, which is why it is credible.

- **Evidence-backed competitive positioning.** Linford's competition answer specifically directs investors to the earnings supplement's merchant fee rate trend chart as empirical proof of pricing stability. This is the opposite of assertion—it is falsifiable, auditable, and treats the investor as capable of verifying the claim independently.

- **Precise condition-setting on strategic optionality.** The bank charter answer ("if there were a feature that we just couldn't build without a bank charter, we would absolutely consider pursuing one") sets a clear, testable threshold rather than leaving the question open-ended. This is a form of accountability—management can be held to this framework in future periods.

- **Credit bureau reporting explanation.** The extended, technically specific discussion of how Affirm worked with TransUnion and Experian to ensure reporting accuracy—including years of testing for "weird consequences"—is a rare instance of a CEO explaining the *work* behind a claim rather than just the claim itself. It communicates both competence and genuine consumer orientation.

### Tracking Notes for Future Evaluations

**Commitments to verify next quarter (Q4 FY2025, approximately August 2025):**

1. **App redesign metrics:** Levchin said "ask me this question maybe next quarter, and you'll be able to ask, hey, you changed this thing and it looks cool." Hold him to specific engagement or conversion metrics from the redesigned app flows, particularly the Affirm Card pre-approval UX changes he specifically flagged.

2. **Shopify Canada beta → UK pathway:** Levchin stated the UK beta will "happen in reasonable time" after Canada beta progress. Next quarter should show either a UK beta announcement or a candid explanation of the delay.

3. **Costco timing:** Described as "some point in a reasonable future." If still unannounced by Q4, ask for updated timeline or signal whether integration complexity has caused revision.

4. **Walmart clarity:** The question will either resolve (announcement of transition/termination) or compound (another quarter of "we're being a good partner"). Either outcome is informative about management's disclosure philosophy.

5. **Zero APR RLTC disclosure:** The market asked this question at least three times in different forms. If the mix remains elevated, management's continued refusal to quantify it should be flagged as a pattern, not a one-quarter omission.

**Structural observation for longitudinal tracking:** This appears to be the first evaluation of Affirm in this framework, so no trend data is available. Future evaluations should track whether the thin prepared-remarks structure is consistent (a deliberate choice relying on letter quality) or whether it reflects declining disclosure investment. The shareholder letter is the load-bearing document at Affirm; its quality should be analyzed alongside each call.
