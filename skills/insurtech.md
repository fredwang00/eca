# Insurance & Insurtech Evaluation Supplement

Supplement to the base Rittenhouse earnings call analysis skill. Layer these criteria on top of the base framework when evaluating insurance and insurtech companies (LMND, ROOT, etc.). These additions do not replace the base dimensions -- they sharpen them for insurance-specific disclosure patterns.

---

## Dimension-Specific Additions

### 1. Capital Stewardship & Financial Candor

- **BVPS is the canonical metric.** Book value per share (or tangible book value per share) is to insurance what ARR is to SaaS. Its absence from an earnings call is a material omission. If management does not discuss BVPS or book value trajectory, flag it explicitly and consider whether the omission is intentional.
- **ROE is the insurance ROIC.** Return on equity is the primary measure of whether an insurer is earning its cost of capital. Track whether it is reported and whether the trend is positive. For growth-stage insurtechs that have not yet achieved positive ROE, track whether management discusses a target or timeline.
- **Non-GAAP cash flow metrics require extra scrutiny** because insurance cash flows are structurally complex: (a) premium collection timing naturally inflates operating cash flow during growth, (b) reinsurance structures can shift cash flows between categories, (c) novel financing mechanisms (synthetic agents, catastrophe bonds, sidecars) can be classified in ways that flatter operating metrics. Always compare non-GAAP cash flow to GAAP operating cash flow and note the gap.
- **Reserve development must be decomposed.** Single-number reserve development (favorable/unfavorable) is insufficient. Demand breakdown by CAT vs. non-CAT and by product line. Favorable non-CAT development is a positive underwriting signal; favorable CAT development may simply reflect conservative initial estimates. Credit companies that decompose; flag those that report only the net number.
- **Combined ratio is the operating margin equivalent.** Loss ratio + expense ratio = combined ratio. Track whether it is reported consistently and whether management provides the components or only the blended number.

### 2. Strategic Clarity & Accountability

- **AI/tech cost advantage claims require evidence in expense ratios.** For insurtechs claiming structural cost advantages through technology, demand evidence in LAE ratios, other insurance expense growth rates vs. premium growth, and automation metrics -- not just assertions about AI capability. Compare the rate of expense growth to premium growth as a basic test.
- **Reinsurance strategy changes are strategic decisions.** Quota share adjustments, retention changes, and new reinsurance structures affect every financial metric on the income statement. They should be explained with clear rationale -- why now, what risk is being transferred or retained, and how it affects reported financials -- not just reported as a fait accompli.
- **Growth-stage tension between premium growth and underwriting quality.** Rapid premium growth inflates cash flows and revenue while potentially masking underwriting problems that emerge only as policies season. Scrutinize whether management acknowledges this dynamic or presents growth metrics in isolation. A company growing premium 30%+ should be expected to discuss loss ratio trends by vintage or cohort, not just blended ratios.

### 3. Stakeholder Balance & Culture Signals

- **Policyholders are the primary customer.** After catastrophe events (wildfires, hurricanes, floods), evaluate whether management discusses policyholder experience -- claims processing speed, customer satisfaction, coverage adequacy -- or only discusses loss ratio impact and financial exposure. The latter treats policyholders as liabilities rather than stakeholders.
- **State regulators are a key stakeholder.** Insurance is regulated at the state level. Track whether regulatory relationships are discussed substantively -- rate filing approvals, market conduct exams, geographic expansion approvals -- or whether regulation is mentioned only as an obstacle or ignored entirely.

### 4. FOG Index -- Linguistic Quality of Disclosure

- **Non-standard metrics require explicit definition.** "Adjusted free cash flow" and similar non-standard metrics must include their construction in the call or shareholder letter. Presenting them without explaining what is included, excluded, or reclassified is FOG by omission -- it creates the appearance of transparency while withholding the information needed to evaluate the metric.
- **Insurance jargon as shield vs. education.** Terms like ceding commissions, quota share, surplus, IBNR, and loss adjustment expense should be explained or contextualized when introduced, not assumed. Credit companies that educate investors on complex insurance mechanics; flag those that deploy jargon without explanation, particularly when the jargon obscures unfavorable dynamics.

### 5. Vision, Leadership & Long-Term Orientation

- **The cost-of-capital question is the ultimate test.** For insurtechs: "when will you earn your cost of capital?" is the long-term orientation question that matters most. Track whether management discusses ROE targets or book value growth as long-term goals, or whether the vision is framed entirely in terms of revenue, EBITDA, and market share -- metrics that can grow while shareholder value erodes.
- **Loss ratio improvement narratives require volume context.** Loss ratios can improve simply by writing less risky business at lower volumes, or by favorable reserve development on prior periods. Demand that management connect loss ratio trends to current-period volume trends and distinguish between current-accident-year and calendar-year loss ratios when possible.

## Red Flags Specific to Insurance

- **Headline cash flow metrics that reclassify financing as operating.** When a company reports "positive adjusted free cash flow" and the construction includes borrowing reclassified from financing activities, the metric is misleading regardless of how well-intentioned. Flag this as a Dim1 and Dim4 issue.
- **Absence of BVPS or book value discussion.** If an insurance company does not discuss book value trajectory on its earnings call, treat it as a material omission equivalent to omitting revenue.
- **Reserve releases used to smooth earnings without acknowledgment.** Favorable prior period development that materially improves the reported loss ratio should be flagged if management presents the blended loss ratio without noting the reserve contribution. This is not fraud -- it is communication that flatters the headline at the expense of clarity.
- **Declining book value alongside growing revenue ("growing yourself poor").** When revenue, premium, and customer count are all growing while book value per share declines, the company may be destroying shareholder value through its growth activities. This pattern requires explicit management acknowledgment and explanation.
- **Reinsurance structure changes presented as financial improvements without discussing the risk transfer trade-off.** Reducing ceded premium increases reported revenue and gross profit but also increases retained risk. If management presents this as purely positive without discussing the volatility trade-off, it is a communication risk.
